The problem

We consume more knowledge than ever, and retain a smaller fraction of it.

Serious readers — researchers, engineers, analysts, lifelong learners — face the same quiet failure. The reading happens. The understanding mostly doesn't stick. Notes scatter across apps, highlights rot in a queue, and the connections between ideas — the part that actually makes you smarter — are never drawn at all.

Fragmentation

Notes are islands

Highlighters, PDFs, doc folders, and note apps all capture fragments. None of them models how a new idea relates to the hundred ideas you already hold. So nothing compounds.

No frontier

You can't see what to learn next

A pile of notes can't tell you where your understanding is thin, what a paper assumes you already know, or which gap is worth closing next given what you're actually trying to figure out.

Dead time

Math-heavy reading doesn't travel

The papers worth reading are exactly the ones you can't skim on a phone or absorb on a commute. Text-to-speech reads equations as gibberish. So the hardest material gets the least attention.

Tools, not partners

Your tools can't act

Even great note apps are passive filing cabinets. They can't connect two ideas for you, draft the missing concept, or maintain the structure as it grows. That work stays manual — so it doesn't happen.

The insight

Treat personal knowledge as a graph asset — and make it operable by an AI agent.

PKIS rests on two bets. First: knowledge should be stored the way it actually works in your head — as a graph of typed concepts and the relationships between them, not as a stack of documents. Second, and newer: that graph should be natively operable by an AI agent, using the exact same read-and-write interface a human uses. The same tools that let you search and connect ideas let Claude extend the graph, draft missing concepts, wire orphaned clusters, and surface what to learn next.

“The unit isn't the document. It's the idea, and its relationships — and the system is something you and an agent grow together.”

That reframing changes the economics of learning. Reading stops being intake and becomes consolidation: each new source is matched against what you already know, deepening existing nodes and flagging the thin ones. Your knowledge stops decaying and starts compounding.

The system

One graph. Many ways in.

PKIS is a structured knowledge graph with an agent-operable API and two clients: a browsable web app and a read-along audio reader. The capabilities below all read and write the same living graph.

🕸️

Typed knowledge graph

Concepts, techniques, results, principles, problems, frameworks and sources — linked by weighted, typed edges (uses, prerequisite-of, contrasts-with…). Structure that a machine can reason over, not just folders.

Foundation
🤖

Agent-native API (MCP)

Durability
Spotlight

The Read+Listen reader: turn a commute into consolidation.

The papers most worth reading are the ones you can't listen to — dense with equations that text-to-speech mangles. PKIS solves this not by reading symbols aloud, but with a semantic narration pass: an LLM explains what the math accomplishes, in plain spoken language, while the real text and rendered equations stay on screen to read along.

  • Hybrid narration. Prose stays faithful to the author's words; math and algorithms get translated for the ear; sparse editorial highlights flag the durable ideas worth remembering.
  • Teaches, doesn't summarize. It motivates each step, names the trade-offs, and connects ideas outward — built to deepen understanding, not just cover ground.
  • Consolidates with your graph. Only PKIS can do this: as concepts appear, the narration calls out your matching nodes and flags the thin ones — “this connects to your coordinate-ascent VI card, which is worth deepening.”
  • Synced read-along. The on-screen section highlights and scrolls with the audio; tap any passage to seek, annotate, or flag a reference into the graph.
  • Built for the phone. Background & lock-screen playback, offline caching, compact MP3 — designed for a real Android commute.
  • Any source. Builds from arXiv, uploaded PDFs (book chapters and papers), or open web articles — one pipeline, three front doors.

How it works

A loop that gets smarter every time you read.

01

Ingest

A paper or chapter enters as a source node — with its file, citation, and a place in the graph.

02

Integrate

An agent extracts the concepts, links them to what you already know, and drafts the nodes that are missing.

03

Surface

The frontier engine ranks what to learn next, weighted toward your active research hypotheses.

04

Consolidate

You read or listen — and the reader weaves your own graph back in, deepening it as you go. Loop.

For the product conversation

Questions worth chewing on.

Who is the second user?

It's purpose-built for one power user. Which adjacent audience — grad students, research teams, technical analysts — feels this pain sharply enough to adopt structure?

Where's the wedge?

The reader is the most immediately lovable, standalone-demoable piece. Is “intelligible audio for hard papers” the wedge that pulls people into the graph?

What's the moat?

The agent-maintained graph compounds with use and is expensive to recreate. Does that personal-data flywheel become defensible, or stay a feature?

Manual vs. magic?

Structure delivers the payoff but costs effort. How much of the graph-building can agents own before the user has to care — and is “zero-effort structure” the real product?
