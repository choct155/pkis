Leveraging Covariance Across the Confusion Matrix

Part A · The tempting mistake

A Flat Joint Dirichlet Is Exactly the Independent Model

It is tempting to think that modeling all four confusion-matrix cells jointly — one Dirichlet over (πTP, πFP, πFN, πTN) instead of two separate Betas — automatically captures the correlation between the true-positive rate and the true-negative rate. It does not. With a flat Dirichlet, the joint model is mathematically identical to the independent one.

The reason is a structural property of the Dirichlet. Recover the rates the natural way — TPR from the cells where A=1, TNR from the cells where A=0:

Recovering the rates from the joint posterior
(πTP, πFP, πFN, πTN) ~ Dirichlet(1+nTP, 1+nFP, 1+nFN, 1+nTN)
TPR = πTP / (πTP + πFN)   // ratio within group A=1 = {TP, FN}
TNR = πTN / (πTN + πFP)   // ratio within group A=0 = {TN, FP}

By the Dirichlet's aggregation / neutrality property — the same additivity that makes coarse- and fine-grained Bayesian inferences consistent — partitioning the cells into the two groups makes the within-group ratios mutually independent, and each one is exactly the Beta posterior you would have written down on its own:

Neutrality → the joint model factorizes
TPR ~ Beta(1+nTP, 1+nFN)   ⊥   TNR ~ Beta(1+nTN, 1+nFP)
Cov(TPR, TNR) = 0    // not assumed — forced, by the flat Dirichlet's structure
The real point A flat prior has no parameter that represents the dependence between the rates — so there is nothing to estimate and nothing to exploit. There is no meat there. The covariance the prose wants is a property of the prior, and a flat prior has set it to zero. Below: the joint cloud lands exactly on the independent cloud, and the correlation reads ≈ 0.

Part B · The real lever

An Explicit Correlational Prior — Where the Gold Savings Come From

The prose was right about the mechanism: the same LLM, on the same documents, drives both error rates, so a capable judge tends to have high TPR and high TNR together. To use that, you have to write it down — put the dependence in the prior, where it can actually do work.

Place a correlated prior on the rates through their log-odds — a shared-capability latent with correlation ρ:

Logistic-normal prior with correlation ρ (keeps the multinomial/Dirichlet likelihood)
( logit TPR, logit TNR ) ~ Normal( μ, Σ ),   Σ = σ²·[[1, ρ],[ρ, 1]]
likelihood:   nTP ~ Binom(nTP+nFN, TPR)  ·  nTN ~ Binom(nTN+nFP, TNR)
// ρ = 0 → exactly the independent model (Part A). ρ > 0 → gold in one group informs the other.

Now ρ is a real, estimable parameter. When prevalence is high you collect almost all your gold in the A=1 group, starving the TNR estimate — but with ρ>0 the well-measured TPR pulls TNR along through the prior. That is the gold saving: the cross-information the flat model threw away.

What ρ buys The correlated cloud is a tilted ellipse — knowing one rate constrains the other — and its marginals are narrower than the independent cloud, most visibly on whichever rate the gold sample starves. Push ρ to 0 and the two clouds coincide (Part A). Push prevalence toward the extremes, where one group is data-starved, and the gold saving grows: the prior correlation is doing the work the flat model couldn't.
