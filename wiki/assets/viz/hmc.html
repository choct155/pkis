Section 1
The Navigation Problem
The typical set is established in PKIS. The question here is sharper: given you know where the mass lives, what does a sampler need to do to stay there — and why does random walk fail at this?

What the typical set demands of a sampler

The typical set is a thin shell in high-dimensional space — thin relative to the full parameter range, but containing essentially all the probability mass. A sampler needs to find it and then traverse it, generating samples that explore the full extent of the shell rather than clustering in one region.

This has two distinct requirements. First, the chain must not drift inward toward the mode or outward into the tails — it must stay on the shell. Second, it must move far enough along the shell on each transition to reduce autocorrelation. A chain that stays on the shell but barely moves achieves correctness but not efficiency.

The geometry of the demand: In d dimensions, the typical set is roughly the surface of a sphere of radius sqrt(d)·σ. A sampler needs to move along that surface, not across it. The surface is (d-1)-dimensional embedded in d-dimensional space — a sampler moving randomly in all d directions is almost guaranteed to move perpendicular to the surface rather than along it.

Why random walk fails — geometrically

Random walk Metropolis proposes a step in a random direction drawn from an isotropic Gaussian. In d dimensions, a random direction is almost surely perpendicular to any fixed surface. The typical set surface has measure zero in the full space — hitting it by chance requires exponentially many tries.

The two failure modes are symmetric and unavoidable. Large steps: proposals land outside the typical set, Metropolis rejects, chain doesn't move. Small steps: proposals stay near the current point, accepts, but traversal of the shell takes O(d) steps because diffusion along a surface of radius sqrt(d) requires O(d) random steps to move distance O(1).

The diffusion bottleneck: Distance covered by a random walk scales as sqrt(N) for N steps. To traverse the typical set shell — diameter ~2·sqrt(d)·σ — requires N ~ d steps. In a 1000-parameter model, you need ~1000 transitions just to cross the shell once. Each transition requires a gradient evaluation. This is why random walk MCMC is unusable for large models.

What coherent exploration looks like

The alternative is a sampler that moves along the shell rather than across it. Distance covered grows linearly with trajectory length, not as its square root. A single trajectory of length T moves distance ~T along the shell. This is what HMC achieves — and it requires knowing the local tangent direction to the typical set, which is exactly what the gradient of the log density encodes.

The key move: The gradient of log π(θ) points toward the mode — perpendicular to the typical set. But if you augment the system with momentum and use the gradient as a force rather than a direction, conservative dynamics automatically produces motion along the shell. This is the Hamiltonian insight.

Section 2
The Hamiltonian Construction
Why augmenting with momentum and defining H = U + K maps energy level sets to probability level sets — exactly, not approximately. This correspondence is the load-bearing move.

The augmentation

Start with target π(θ). Introduce auxiliary momentum p, one variable per dimension of θ. The joint distribution is defined to factor:

Joint Distribution
π(θ, p) = π(θ) · π(p)
// p chosen independent of θ — this factoring is deliberate
// π(p) = N(0, M) — Gaussian with mass matrix M

Take the negative log of both sides. Define U and K as the two terms:

Defining U and K
U(θ) = −log π(θ) ← potential energy
K(p) = −log π(p) = ½·pTM-1p ← kinetic energy

H(θ, p) = U(θ) + K(p) = −log π(θ, p)
H is not a metaphor. The Hamiltonian is literally the negative log of the joint density. This is a definition, not an analogy to physics. The physics analogy is a useful intuition pump, but the mathematical identity H = −log π(θ,p) is exact and is what makes the Metropolis correction work.

The level set correspondence

A level set of H is the set of (θ,p) pairs where H takes a fixed value c. Constant H means constant −log π(θ,p) means constant π(θ,p). So level sets of H are exactly level sets of the joint density.

Now: the typical set of π(θ) is the shell where probability mass concentrates. For the joint distribution π(θ,p), the typical set is a shell in the higher-dimensional (θ,p) space. Conservative Hamiltonian dynamics — which exactly conserves H — keeps trajectories exactly on these level sets.

The correspondence: Staying on a level set of H = staying on a level set of π(θ,p) = staying in the typical set of the joint distribution. Marginalizing over p gives samples from π(θ). The energy surface and the typical set are the same object.

Why U(θ) = −log π(θ) specifically

Any other choice of U would target the wrong distribution. If U = π(θ) (the density itself rather than its negative log), the level sets of H would not correspond to level sets of the target. The negative log transform is what makes the energy and probability representations commensurate.

1
Marginalizing out p recovers π(θ). Since π(θ,p) = π(θ)·π(p) by construction, integrating over p gives ∫π(θ,p)dp = π(θ)·∫π(p)dp = π(θ). Samples from the joint distribution, projected onto θ, are exactly distributed as π(θ).
2
The Gaussian kinetic energy is not arbitrary. K(p) = ½pᵀM⁻¹p gives π(p) ∝ exp(−K(p)) = Gaussian. This means momentum can be drawn independently at each step from a known distribution. Any other kinetic energy would require sampling from a non-standard distribution.
3
M sets the length scale. The mass matrix M controls how fast the momentum variable moves relative to position. Optimal M matches the covariance of π(θ) — this is why preconditioning matters and why the Euclidean metric is a special case.

Section 3
Why the Hamiltonian Construction is Unique
Symplecticity and time-reversibility are not properties HMC happens to have — they are requirements that uniquely force the Hamiltonian structure. This section builds the necessity argument.

What any valid MCMC transition requires

For a Markov chain to target π, its transitions must satisfy detailed balance. For a deterministic proposal map f (like integrating dynamics), detailed balance reduces to two conditions:

Requirements on Deterministic Proposals
(1) Volume preservation: |det J_f| = 1
→ Jacobian of the map = 1 exactly
→ Required for acceptance ratio = π(f(x))/π(x)

(2) Time-reversibility: f(f(x,p), −p) = (x, p)
→ Running backward returns to start
→ Required for proposal ratio Q(B→A)/Q(A→B) = 1

Now add a third requirement: the proposal must use gradient information to generate coherent motion along the typical set rather than random diffusion.

The three requirements: volume preservation + time-reversibility + gradient-driven coherent exploration. Any map satisfying all three must have Hamiltonian structure. This is not a theorem we prove here — but we can see why each alternative fails.

Alternatives and their failure modes

Gradient descent
Follows gradient directly. Not volume-preserving (contracts toward attractor). Not time-reversible. Correct direction but wrong dynamics — crashes into mode.
Gradient ascent on π
Moves toward mode faster. Catastrophically contracts volume. No mechanism to orbit the typical set. Fails requirement 1 severely.
Overdamped Langevin
Adds noise to gradient. Time-reversible with MH correction. But diffusive — distance scales as sqrt(N). Same scaling as random walk. No coherent orbit.
Underdamped Langevin (MALA)
Closer — momentum-like variable. But adds friction that breaks exact volume preservation. Requires MH correction that includes Jacobian terms. More complex than HMC.

Why Hamiltonian dynamics satisfies all three

Liouville's theorem: any system governed by Hamilton's equations exactly preserves phase space volume. This is a theorem of classical mechanics that follows directly from the structure of Hamilton's equations — the divergence of the Hamiltonian vector field is exactly zero.

Time-reversibility: Hamilton's equations are symmetric under t → −t combined with p → −p. Running the system backward with negated momentum exactly retraces the trajectory.

Coherent motion: the Hamiltonian vector field is tangent to the energy level sets — by definition. Trajectories flow along constant-H surfaces, which are the typical set surfaces.

The uniqueness argument: You need a deterministic map that (1) preserves volume, (2) is time-reversible, (3) moves along level sets of a function related to the target. Hamiltonian dynamics is the canonical structure that satisfies all three simultaneously. Other approaches satisfy at most two.

Symplecticity is stronger than volume preservation

Liouville gives volume preservation. But leapfrog preserves something stronger: the symplectic form, a two-form on phase space that encodes the area of all two-dimensional projections. Symplectic maps preserve these areas exactly, not just the total volume. This is what makes the Jacobian calculation exact rather than approximate.

Intuition: A non-symplectic volume-preserving map could rotate and shear phase space, preserving total volume while distorting shape. A symplectic map preserves the shape of every 2D cross-section. This stronger property is what gives |J| = 1 exactly for leapfrog, not just approximately.

Section 4
Leapfrog from First Principles
Why this specific three-part update — and not Euler, Runge-Kutta, or anything else. Built up from the requirements established in Section 3.

What any integrator must satisfy

Continuous Hamiltonian dynamics satisfies volume preservation and time-reversibility exactly. A numerical integrator approximates the dynamics with finite steps — and introduces errors. The question is which integrator preserves the structural properties that make Metropolis correction work.

Requirements on the Integrator
Symplectic: |J_f| = 1 exactly for each step
→ so the Jacobian cancels in the MH ratio

Time-reversible: f(f(θ,p),−p) = (θ,p)
→ so the proposal ratio Q(B→A)/Q(A→B) = 1

Consistent: error → 0 as step size → 0
→ accuracy in the limit (but not required for correctness!)

Accuracy is not a correctness requirement. You can have large energy errors and still get exact samples, because Metropolis corrects for them. What you cannot compromise is symplecticity and reversibility.

Why Euler fails

The Euler method updates both θ and p in a single step using current-point gradients:

Euler Update
θ_new = θ + ε·p
p_new = p − ε·∇U(θ) ← uses old θ gradient

The Jacobian of this map is:

Euler Jacobian
J = [[1, ε], [−ε·∇²U, 1−ε²·∇²U]]
det(J) = 1 − ε²·∇²U(θ)
≠ 1 in general ← volume not preserved

// Contracts if ∇²U > 0 (bowl-shaped potential)
// Error accumulates: det(J^n) = (1 − ε²·∇²U)^n → 0
Euler with Metropolis correction: Technically works — you include the Jacobian in the acceptance ratio. But now α = min(1, π(end)/π(start) · |J|⁻¹) — and evaluating |J| requires the Hessian ∇²U, which costs O(d²) per step. For large d this is catastrophic. Leapfrog's unit Jacobian eliminates this cost entirely.

Why Runge-Kutta fails

Runge-Kutta methods (RK4 is the standard) achieve high accuracy by evaluating the gradient at intermediate points within each step. But they are not symplectic — the intermediate evaluations break the exact cancellation of mixed partials that gives volume preservation.

RK4 is more accurate but not symplectic. Over long trajectories, its energy error drifts systematically rather than oscillating. With Metropolis correction, drift causes rejection rates to grow with trajectory length — exactly the opposite of what you want. Leapfrog's oscillating (non-drifting) error is fundamentally better suited to the MH framework.

Building leapfrog from shear maps

The key insight: a map that updates only one variable while holding the other fixed is always a shear map, and shear maps are always volume-preserving with Jacobian 1.

Half-step in p (shear in p-direction)
p_{1/2} = p − (ε/2)·∇U(θ) ← θ unchanged
// Jacobian: [[1,0],[−(ε/2)·∇²U, 1]] → det = 1 ✓
Full step in θ (shear in θ-direction)
θ_new = θ + ε·p_{1/2} ← p unchanged
// Jacobian: [[1, ε],[0, 1]] → det = 1 ✓
Half-step in p again (shear in p-direction)
p_new = p_{1/2} − (ε/2)·∇U(θ_new) ← θ_new used
// Jacobian: det = 1 ✓

// Composition of three det=1 maps:
det(J_leapfrog) = 1 × 1 × 1 = 1 ✓
Why the half-steps? Time-reversibility requires the sequence to be palindromic — the same steps in reverse order with negated momentum must return to the start. Half-step / full-step / half-step is the minimal palindromic structure. A full-step / full-step scheme is not time-reversible.

Energy error behavior: oscillation vs drift

Leapfrog energy error oscillates around the true value — it doesn't accumulate. This is a consequence of symplecticity: the map preserves the symplectic form, so there's no systematic direction for energy to drift. Euler and RK4 errors drift, which causes acceptance rates to degrade with trajectory length.

Section 5
Why Metropolis Faithfully Corrects Numerical Error
Three nested layers: accept-reject on biased proposals produces exact samples → H is the right density ratio → symplecticity makes the correction clean.

Layer 1: Detailed balance and exact sampling

A Markov chain targets distribution π if and only if it satisfies detailed balance: for any two states A and B, the probability flow from A to B equals the flow from B to A.

Detailed Balance
π(A) · T(A→B) = π(B) · T(B→A)
// flow A→B = flow B→A for all pairs
// If this holds, π is stationary → long-run samples are exactly π

Metropolis-Hastings constructs T to satisfy this by design. With proposal Q and acceptance α:

MH Acceptance (satisfies DB by construction)
α(A→B) = min(1, π(B)·Q(B→A) / π(A)·Q(A→B))

// Substituting into flow A→B = π(A)·Q(A→B)·α(A→B):
flow A→B = min(π(A)·Q(A→B), π(B)·Q(B→A))
flow B→A = min(π(B)·Q(B→A), π(A)·Q(A→B))
→ always equal, for any Q
The proposal can be arbitrarily biased. Detailed balance holds regardless of what Q does. Q only affects efficiency — how often you accept and how far you move. Correctness requires only the ability to evaluate π(A), π(B), Q(A→B), Q(B→A).

Layer 2: H as the exact density ratio

HMC operates in joint (θ,p) space. The target in joint space is π(θ,p) = π(θ)·π(p). Since H = −log π(θ,p), the Metropolis ratio becomes:

MH Ratio in Joint Space
α = min(1, π(θ*,p*) / π(θ,p))

= min(1, exp(−H(θ*,p*)) / exp(−H(θ,p)))

= min(1, exp(−ΔH))

// ΔH = H(end) − H(start) = accumulated leapfrog error
// If leapfrog exact: ΔH = 0, α = 1, always accept
// If leapfrog drifts: ΔH > 0, α < 1, reject proportionally
The correction is exact regardless of ΔH's size. Whether the leapfrog error is 0.001 or 10, exp(−ΔH) is exactly the right acceptance probability. Large step sizes create large ΔH and frequent rejection — correct but inefficient. Small step sizes create small ΔH and high acceptance — correct and efficient.

Layer 3: Why symplecticity makes the ratio clean

The full MH ratio for a deterministic map f includes a Jacobian term:

Full MH Ratio for Deterministic Proposal
α = min(1, π(f(x)) / π(x) · |det J_f(x)|)

// For leapfrog: |det J_f| = 1 exactly
// Jacobian term vanishes
→ α = min(1, exp(−ΔH)) ← no Hessian needed

// For Euler: |det J_f| ≠ 1
// Need Hessian ∇²U — O(d²) cost
→ α = min(1, exp(−ΔH) · |J|⁻¹)
The full chain of necessity:
Leapfrog is symplectic → |J| = 1 → Jacobian cancels → α = exp(−ΔH) → exact correction for all numerical error → exact samples from π. Remove symplecticity and the correction becomes intractable.

Section 7
Neal's Funnel — Where Geometry Breaks HMC
The funnel is where all four properties we've built break down simultaneously. Understanding why it fails deepens everything above.

The funnel distribution

Neal's funnel is a two-dimensional distribution: v ~ N(0,3) and θ₁ ~ N(0, exp(v)). The variable v sets the scale of θ₁. When v is large and positive, θ₁ spreads wide. When v is large and negative, θ₁ is extremely tight near zero.

This structure arises naturally in hierarchical models — v plays the role of a hyperparameter (a group-level variance), and θ₁ is a data-level parameter whose scale depends on v. Real Bayesian hierarchical models have this geometry.

The curvature problem: The gradient of U(v,θ₁) changes dramatically across the funnel. At v = 2 (wide neck), the curvature ∂²U/∂θ₁² = exp(−v) ≈ 0.1 — shallow. At v = −3 (narrow neck), curvature = exp(3) ≈ 20 — extremely steep. A step size that works at the top shreds the energy surface at the bottom.

How this breaks leapfrog

The optimal leapfrog step size scales roughly as 1/sqrt(curvature). In the wide top, curvature ≈ 0.1, so ε can be ~3. In the narrow bottom, curvature ≈ 20, so ε must be ~0.2. A single fixed ε cannot satisfy both.

With ε tuned for the top: trajectories in the narrow neck accumulate catastrophic energy errors. ΔH >> 0. Metropolis rejects. The chain cannot explore the neck region at all.

Divergent transitions: When ΔH is astronomically large (say > 1000), the leapfrog trajectory has left the energy surface entirely. Stan and PyMC flag these as divergences — not just rejected proposals, but evidence of a fundamental geometry problem. The red X markers in the visualization below are exactly these.

Why divergences are informative

A random walk chain getting stuck near the funnel neck would simply have low effective sample size — you'd see no warning. HMC with divergences gives you a positive signal: the integrator detected geometry that standard methods miss. The location of divergences even tells you where the problematic region is.

The fix: non-centered parameterization

Instead of modeling θ₁ ~ N(0, exp(v)) directly (centered), reparameterize: let z ~ N(0,1) and set θ₁ = z·exp(v/2). The posterior over (v, z) has nearly constant curvature — no funnel. HMC with standard step size works perfectly.

What this does geometrically: The non-centered parameterization flattens the energy surface so that curvature is roughly constant across the space. The typical set is still a shell, but the shell's local geometry is uniform. A single step size works everywhere.
