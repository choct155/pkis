Stage 1 · What you already built

The PPI Rectifier — Your Debiasing, Named

Prediction-Powered Inference (Angelopoulos, Bates, Fannjiang, Jordan, Zrnic, Science 2023) is the formal frequentist framework for exactly the move Tabs 1–2 make: estimate a quantity from a large pile of cheap predictions, then correct it with a small trusted sample. The estimand here is the accuracy p; the "silver" predictor is the LLM judgment L; the gold is the human label Y.

The PPI estimator — silver estimate, minus its measured bias on the gold
p̂PPI = (1/N) Σsilver Li − [ (1/n) Σgold Lj − (1/n) Σgold Yj ]
// silver mean over everything, minus the rectifier Δ = E[L − Y] measured on the gold subset

The bracketed term is the rectifier: the LLM's average over-/under-statement, estimated on the cases where you have ground truth. Tab 1's confusion-matrix inversion p̂ = (P(L=1) − FPR)/(TPR − FPR) is this same correction written through the rates instead of the means — algebraically the same debiasing, which is why PPI is the literature your framework already lives in.

The link to separation The covariance that makes the silver useful is Cov(L, Y) = p(1−p)·(TPR − FPR). The very same separation TPR − FPR that governed Tab 1's break-even and Tab 2's correlation gain is what gives PPI its power. When it collapses, so does everything.
Stage 2 · The fix for "not always worth it"

PPI++ — Power-Tuning λ, and the Guarantee You're Never Worse Than Gold

Plain PPI has a failure mode — the one your Recap flagged as "the overlay is not always worth it." When the judge is weak, the rectifier is noisier than the thing it corrects, and PPI can be worse than just hand-labeling the gold. PPI++ (Angelopoulos, Duchi, Zrnic 2023) closes that gap with a single coefficient λ that down-weights the silver by exactly how much it deserves:

PPI++ — one tuning coefficient λ ∈ [0, 1]
p̂λ = λ·(1/N) Σsilver Li + (1/n) Σgold ( Yj − λ·Lj )
λ* = Cov(L, Y) / [ Var(L)·(1 + n/N) ]   // the variance-minimizing weight
// λ = 0 → gold-only. λ = 1 → plain PPI. λ* → optimal, and never worse than gold-only.

Because λ = 0 recovers the gold-only estimator and λ* is chosen to minimize variance, PPI++ is provably at least as good as gold-only — for any judge quality. When separation collapses, λ* slides to 0 and PPI++ gracefully becomes gold-only. Your break-even caveat becomes: with λ-tuning, there is no break-even — the hybrid never loses.

Stage 3 · Where it still needs you

The Blindspot, and How the Three Tabs Fit

PPI corrects a scalar bias. It cannot, on its own, see a symmetric judge — one whose false positives and false negatives cancel in the mean. The rectifier reads zero; the estimate looks unbiased while the judge is useless. The confusion-matrix view (Tab 1) and the joint model (Tab 2) catch this by construction, because they track the cells, not just the net. The separation TPR − FPR is the quantity to monitor, and it is the same quantity λ* keys off.

The arc across the three tabs Tab 1 builds the confusion-matrix inversion (the rectifier, through the rates). Tab 2 adds the correlational prior that buys gold from shared judge capability. Tab 3 names the formal estimator and adds λ-tuning — the guarantee that the hybrid is never worse than gold. Remaining work, where you take over: extending λ* to the confusion-matrix estimand directly, rather than the mean.
