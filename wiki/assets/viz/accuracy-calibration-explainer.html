The Task

The Extraction Problem — Headline Financials from SEC Filings

Every year, public companies in the US file a 10-K with the SEC — an annual financial report that includes three core statements: the income statement, the balance sheet, and the cash flow statement. We run a production pipeline that extracts headline fields from the income statement — revenue, EBITDA, earnings — across thousands of these filings.

This is a data-manufacturing process, and like any such process it is never perfectly accurate. Every extraction carries some nonzero probability of error: a misread figure, the wrong line item, a units or sign mistake. To know the true error rate, we have always done the same thing: pull a representative sample of extractions, have people review them by hand, and estimate accuracy from that sample. The question was never whether to sample — it's that human review is expensive. Each estimate is an offline pass: assemble the sample, route it to reviewers, wait. We can only afford so much of it, so the size of the sample — and therefore the precision of our estimate — is capped by how much human time we are willing to spend.

To get a more precise estimate without paying for more human review, we move from one sampling regime to two — a hybrid. An LLM does a broad pass over the full representative sample (silver data — cheap, plentiful, but noisy), while humans review only a smaller subset of that same sample (gold data — our trusted standard). The key assumption: humans are the gold standard, and the LLM, useful as it is, makes errors more often than they do.

The LLM renders a binary judgment on each extraction in its pass: L=1 if it deems the extraction correct, L=0 if incorrect. Because the LLM is itself imperfect, we can't take that silver label at face value — we use the gold subset to measure how often the LLM is right, then debias our accuracy estimate accordingly. That calibration is what the confusion matrix below sets up.

Why this matters The constraint was never whether to sample — it's that human review is the bottleneck on precision. The hybrid silver/gold design lets us scale the sample cheaply with the LLM, then spend a small, well-chosen gold budget to debias the result. The rest of this explainer makes that tradeoff — precision versus gold cost — a quantity you can see.

The confusion matrix records how often L agrees with A across all four possible outcomes. Crucially, we do not directly observe the agreements and disagreements between L and A — A itself is hidden.

Stage 2 of 3

Prevalence Estimation — Debiasing with the Gold Sample

We now want to estimate the prevalence p — but as we saw in Stage 1, p is a function of quantities we never directly observe. The one thing we do observe from the silver pass is how often the LLM says an extraction is correct: the LLM positive rate P̂(L=1). What we cannot see are the LLM's error characteristics — its true positive rate (TPR) and false positive rate (FPR).

The derivation below shows precisely why those two are what we need: given the observed P̂(L=1), the TPR and FPR are exactly enough to back out p. Since we can't observe them, we have to estimate them — and that is what the gold sample is for: a small, human-verified subset of the same representative sample, used to pin down the TPR and FPR so we can debias the prevalence estimate.

The prevalence estimation formula — derivation
P̂(L=1) = P(L=1 | A=1) · P(A=1) + P(L=1 | A=0) · P(A=0)
P̂(L=1) = TPR · p + (1 − TNR) · (1 − p)
// rearrange for p:
p̂ = ( P̂(L=1) − (1 − TNR) ) / ( TPR − (1 − TNR) )
// equivalently, with FPR = 1 − TNR:
p̂ = ( P̂(L=1) − FPR ) / ( TPR − FPR )
// the standard ROC-based prevalence estimator

With TPR and FPR known exactly, p̂ recovers the true p exactly — the inversion is purely algebraic. Estimation error only enters once TPR and FPR must themselves be estimated from a finite gold sample, which is Stage 3.
The identification problem This formula requires knowing the true TPR and TNR — which come from the gold sample. If the gold sample is too small, the confusion matrix estimates are noisy, and the prevalence estimate inherits that noise. This is what we fix in Stage 3.
Computing prevalence with uncertainty

Stage 3 of 3

Bayesian Updating — How Each Gold Observation Shifts Your Uncertainty

Before seeing any gold observations, you have prior uncertainty over TPR and TNR. Each gold observation — a human judgment H on a case where you also have L — updates your posterior beliefs about those parameters. Watch the distributions sharpen as you add observations.

We model each rate with a Beta distribution — the natural conjugate prior for a yes/no success probability — so every gold observation simply increments one of its two parameters, as the matrix below shows.

The surprise principle The most informative observation is the one you least expect. When the LLM looks highly accurate (TPR near 1), another TP barely moves your belief — but an FN is surprising and shifts it a lot. Because the rare outcome for each rate is usually a disagreement between LLM and human, deliberately seeking out discordant cases (stratified sampling) buys more bits per gold dollar than sampling at random.
Why information is never negative Surprisal is always ≥ 0 — a single observation can never carry negative information. You might worry that a case which contradicts a confident belief makes you more uncertain. Seeing one such case can momentarily widen a single posterior, but averaged over which outcome you actually draw, the expected information is non-negative: you cannot expect to be made more uncertain just by looking. The right per-observation measure is therefore the surprisal, which is non-negative by construction.
How much gold (and silver) do you actually need?

At a small gold sample the Beta(2,2) prior shrinks the rate estimates toward 0.5 and the inversion inflates p̂ (that is why a representative sample of 24 still misses 0.75). Hold the gold sample representative — cell shares fixed at the data-generating process — and scale it up: as the data overwhelms the prior, the p̂ posterior collapses onto the true prevalence. This is the budgeting tool. Pick the precision you need; read off the gold.

Gold is the binding constraint; silver sets the floor Silver is the cheap LLM pass — make it large; its only job is to pin down P̂(L=1), and that precision is nearly free. Gold is expensive human labor estimating the LLM's error rates, so it sets your budget. But silver imposes a floor: with a small silver set P̂(L=1) is itself noisy, and no amount of gold can push p̂'s interval below the grey band. Buy enough silver to drop the floor under your target, then buy the gold to reach it. Notice too that the curve depends on the LLM's separation (TPR − FPR) from Stage 1 — a sharper LLM needs far less gold.
Is the LLM overlay even worth it?

Two ways to estimate prevalence to your target precision. Hybrid: the cheap silver pass plus a gold subset to debias it — what this whole explainer builds. Gold-only: skip the LLM entirely and hand-label a random sample, then just average — the expensive procedure you would otherwise run. Gold-only's cost is fixed; the hybrid's gold cost depends on how good the LLM is. So: how accurate must the judge be before the overlay pays for itself?

The instability as TPR − FPR → 0 Every hybrid quantity divides by the separation TPR − FPR (= TPR + TNR − 1). As the judge approaches chance, that denominator collapses toward zero — so the prevalence estimate, its credible interval, and the sensitivities above all blow up, and the gold required runs to infinity. A judge that barely separates correct from incorrect extractions cannot be debiased at any feasible cost; the silver pass only adds noise the gold can never undo. Separation, not headline accuracy, is the quantity to watch. (These derivatives are also local — near that edge a single first-order number stops being meaningful.)
What you're buying — and when The LLM overlay is not free: the hybrid spends gold to estimate the LLM's own error rates (TPR and FPR), and FPR is learned from only the minority of cases that are truly wrong. Below the break-even accuracy a*, that overhead exceeds the savings and you would be better off just hand-labeling a random sample. Above a*, the silver pass replaces most of the human work and the gold needed plummets. The bar a* rises as you demand more precision or starve the silver set — so "should we use an LLM judge here?" has a quantitative answer that depends on your LLM, your target, and your silver budget.
What moves the budget — and the remaining assumptions Beyond the separation effect above, two more levers are visible in the simulation: FPR (= 1 − TNR) is learned only from the truly-wrong minority, so at high prevalence it is the noisiest input and usually the binding one; and the silver floor caps precision no matter how much gold you buy, while tightening the target raises gold steeply.

This curve makes no symmetry assumption and uses no Gaussian approximation — TPR and TNR are set independently and p̂'s interval comes straight from the Monte-Carlo draws (the same machinery as the budget tool above). Skew the two sliders apart and watch the asymmetry move the break-even a*. The assumptions that remain are honest and few: a representative gold sample (cell shares match the true rates), a weak Beta(2,2) prior on each rate, and independent silver and gold draws.
Recap

What we've learned

The task was to estimate one number — the true accuracy of a production extraction pipeline — without paying humans to check every case. The hybrid silver/gold design does it, but only under conditions you can now state precisely.

  • The LLM's accuracy is a marginal. It blends TPR and TNR weighted by prevalence; two very different error structures yield the same headline number, so you cannot recover the true rate from the silver pass alone.
  • Debiasing is confusion-matrix inversion. p̂ = (P̂(L=1) − FPR)/(TPR − FPR). The cheap silver pass gives you P̂(L=1); a small gold subset gives you TPR and FPR.
  • Uncertainty in, uncertainty out. TPR and FPR are themselves estimated, so p̂ is a distribution, not a point — and a weak prior biases it at small gold sizes until the data overwhelms the prior.
  • Not all gold is equal. The information a case carries is its surprisal; rare disagreements (FP, FN) are worth the most, so stratified sampling beats sampling at random.
  • Silver sets a floor; gold sets the budget. Silver is cheap — buy enough to pin down P̂(L=1); gold is the expensive lever that buys precision, and you can read off how much you need.
  • The overlay is not always worth it. Below a break-even LLM accuracy, the hybrid costs more gold than just hand-labeling a random sample. What governs this is the separation TPR − FPR: as it shrinks toward zero the inversion turns unstable and no amount of gold can rescue it.

The transferable mindset: when you swap human effort for a cheaper machine, don't ask "is it good enough?" — calibrate it, debias with a small gold sample, and quantify the precision-versus-cost tradeoff before you trust it. That is how you lower the cost of measurement without fooling yourself.
