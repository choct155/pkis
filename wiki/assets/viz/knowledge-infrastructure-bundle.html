The problem

Your AI system is paying full price on every question.

Document retrieval and inference-time synthesis work. The problem is they never get cheaper. Each answer costs the same as the last — full document consumption, full synthesis, no memory of the work done.

Consider a question any analyst in your organization might ask: "What was the reported NAV for Carlyle's flagship buyout fund last quarter, and how does that compare to their disclosed fair value methodology?"

That question requires understanding what NAV is and how it's calculated, what fair value methodology means under ASC 820, how Carlyle's specific fund reports it, and what the actual reported figure was. Without structure, your system retrieves several documents, reads most of each, synthesizes the conceptual connections at inference time, and returns an answer. Next week, someone asks a related question. The system does it again — same documents, same synthesis, same cost.

Nothing compounded. Nothing was remembered. You paid twice.

Common objections

We already do something like this.

Most organizations have taken steps to improve retrieval. Each step helps — but each leaves a residual gap that only structure resolves.

Anticipated pushback — click to expand
Q "We use chunked embeddings, not full documents. Doesn't that solve the token waste problem?" ▶

Chunking improves things. A smaller chunk costs fewer tokens to retrieve and process, so the efficiency ratio improves. But chunk boundaries are determined by token count or sentence windows — not by concept boundaries. A chunk might contain half an explanation of fair value methodology and half a discussion of reporting timelines. The embedding captures a blend. The system still pays for irrelevant content within each chunk, and still has to synthesize across chunks at inference time.

More importantly: chunks don't know what they contain. Two chunks discussing NAV methodology in different documents have no structural link. The system has to rediscover that connection on every query. A concept node makes that connection once.

Verdict: Chunking narrows the efficiency gap but doesn't close it. The fundamental mismatch — chunk granularity vs. concept granularity — persists.
Q "We have a Solr index with document-level metadata. That's fast retrieval already." ▶

A metadata index is fast retrieval to the right document. But document-level metadata — issuer, document type, date received, fund name — answers "which document?" not "what does this document say about X?" The conceptual content of the document is still locked inside it. Once you retrieve the document, you're back to full synthesis.

Metadata indexing solves the navigation problem, not the coverage problem. You still pay full inference cost on every question that requires understanding the document's content, not just locating it.

Verdict: Metadata indexing is a retrieval filter, not a knowledge representation. It gets you to the document faster but doesn't reduce what you pay once you're there.
Q "What if we enrich our document metadata with concept and entity references from a shared vocabulary?" ▶

This is the closest partial solution and it moves the needle meaningfully. If documents are tagged with canonical concept identifiers, you can retrieve by concept without scanning document content. That addresses the navigation problem for concepts, not just administrative metadata.

But enriched metadata is still set membership: this document mentions {NAV methodology, fair value, vintage effects}. It tells you the concepts are present but nothing about how they relate. Is NAV methodology a prerequisite for understanding fair value here, or a consequence? Is the fair value discussion explanatory or merely referential? The relationship is lost.

Without directed typed relationships between concepts, you can't traverse dependency chains. You can't say "to answer this question, you need to understand X before Y." You get better recall, not better reasoning about what to retrieve next.

Verdict: Enriched metadata is the best document-centric approach and is worth doing. It still lacks directionality and typed relationships — the information content of graph edges is not recoverable from tags.
Q "Bigger models handle synthesis better. Why invest in structure when the models keep improving?" ▶

Better models improve synthesis quality. They don't change the fundamental cost structure: every query pays full inference cost, synthesis is repeated from scratch, nothing compounds. A 10x better model at the same query volume costs the same as a 1x model — it's just more accurate at the full price.

Structure and model quality are complements, not substitutes. Structure reduces the work the model has to do at inference time. A better model on top of good structure is strictly better than a better model on top of no structure — and cheaper per answer.

Verdict: Model improvement is not an architectural substitute. Better models reduce error rates on synthesis; structure reduces the number of times synthesis is needed.
The architecture

Two separable layers. Independent value. Compounding returns.

The concept and instance layers address different parts of the query space. Each can be implemented independently. Together they cover the full range of questions your organization asks.

Layer 1 — Concept graph
What things are and how they relate
Encodes definitions, methodologies, frameworks, and the typed relationships between them. Answers conceptual questions without document retrieval. Prerequisite relationships are explicit: understanding fair value requires understanding Level 2/3 inputs.
Example nodes
NAV methodology Fair value (ASC 820) Mark-to-market lag Vintage effects Illiquidity premium
Layer 2 — Instance graph
Who specific entities are and what they hold
Encodes canonical entity identities, their properties, and their relationships. Resolves the same entity across different naming conventions. Answers factual questions as structured lookups rather than document scans.
Example nodes
Carlyle Group [GP] CCP V [Fund] JPMorgan AM [LP] NAV Q3 2024: $4.2B Commitment: $200M

The two layers answer different questions. "What is NAV methodology?" — concept layer. "What was Carlyle Fund V's reported NAV last quarter?" — instance layer. "How does Carlyle's fair value methodology affect their reported NAV?" — both layers, traversed together. The query scope determines which layers are needed. Both can be queried independently.

Critically, the layers are also separable in implementation. Building the concept layer first delivers immediate value on conceptual questions and establishes the vocabulary that makes the instance layer more precise. The instance layer builds on that foundation — but it doesn't require it. Either can be implemented first based on where the highest query volume sits.

The break-even point depends on construction cost per node and your query volume. At conservative estimates — 2,500 tokens per document retrieval, 220 tokens per concept node, 1,400 tokens to construct a node — the system breaks even in under 20 queries per node. Most organizations ask hundreds of questions per concept per month.

The instance layer has a similar break-even structure but with higher construction cost and higher per-query savings — fact retrieval replaces not just document consumption but also the extraction and normalization work that happens every time the same entity appears in a different document.

Design principles

Three principles that guide the build.

1
Harden what gets used, not what seems important
The signal for what to encode is query frequency, not perceived conceptual centrality. Build structure where your organization's questions land repeatedly. Let inference handle the long tail. The graph grows where the demand is.
2
Separate what something is from what it is called
Canonical entity identity is the foundation of the instance layer. JPMorgan Asset Management, JPMorgan AM, and a specific JPMorgan line of business are one entity or three, depending on whether you've resolved it. Without canonical identity, every query over entity relationships returns incomplete and inconsistent results.
3
Structure and inference are complements, not competitors
The goal is not to replace model inference with structure. It is to reserve inference for the questions that genuinely require it, and let structure handle everything else. The model's job becomes synthesis over a well-prepared substrate, not reconstruction of relationships it should have been able to look up.
The argument runs deeper.

The efficiency claim is grounded in information theory and has a formal derivation. The interactive example walks through the Carlyle NAV query step by step — concept retrieval, instance retrieval, and the residual gap that document-based approaches leave.

Primitives

We establish the formal objects required for the derivation. All subsequent results follow from these definitions.

q A natural language query posed to the system.
𝒞 The universe of all concepts — both those encoded in the graph and those recoverable only from world knowledge.
C(q) A probability distribution over 𝒞 induced by q. P(c | q) is the weight of concept c in answering q — the fraction of q's information need attributable to c. Formally: Σc ∈ 𝒞 P(c | q) = 1.
G A multipartite knowledge graph whose nodes include typed concept nodes, source nodes, and relational edges.
n A retrieved node — either a concept node c ∈ G or a document node d ∈ G.
tokens(n) The token cost of consuming node n — proportional to its length in the context window.
I(q ; n) The mutual information between query q and node n: the expected reduction in uncertainty about C(q) obtained by retrieving n.
H(q | S) Residual entropy about q given retrieved set S — how much of C(q) remains uncovered.
ε Sufficiency threshold: retrieval terminates when marginal information gain ΔI(q ; n | S) < ε.
Kc Graph construction cost per concept node — token cost of encoding a concept node at integration time.
N Query volume — number of queries over the amortization horizon.
Note on C(q) C(q) is not directly observable. In practice it is approximated via semantic bridging — mapping query tokens to candidate concept nodes by embedding-space distance. This approximation problem is treated as separable from the coverage computation and deferred. The derivation below conditions on a well-estimated C(q).
Coverage of a Query by a Node Set

Define the coverage of query q by graph G as the expected mass of C(q) that falls on encoded nodes:

Definition — Coverage
Coverage(q, G) = Σc ∈ G P(c | q) · 𝟙[c ∈ G]

// Each concept node is a Bernoulli trial against C(q).
// Mass on un-encoded concepts must be recovered at inference time — at full model cost.

Retrieval proceeds greedily by marginal information gain. Given a retrieved set S, the marginal gain from adding node n is:

Marginal Information Gain
ΔI(q ; n | S) = I(q ; n, S) − I(q ; S)

// Add nodes in descending order of ΔI until ΔI < ε.
// ε is the sufficiency threshold — a design parameter.

The retrieved set S* at termination satisfies H(q | S*) < ε. Any mass of C(q) not covered by S* is handled at synthesis time — either recovered from model world knowledge or lost.

Document Retrieval: The Token Waste Problem

A document d is a multi-concept artifact. Its information content spans many concepts simultaneously:

Document information structure
P(k | d) = distribution over knowledge units contained in d

// P(k | d) is broad: most knowledge units in d are irrelevant to q.
// But tokens(d) is paid in full regardless of relevance.

The mutual information between q and document d decomposes as:

Document mutual information
I(q ; d) = I(q ; d)relevant + I(q ; d)irrelevant

// Only the relevant portion contributes to Coverage(q, G).
// The irrelevant portion costs tokens but yields zero mutual information with q.

Define the relevance ratio of document d with respect to query q:

Relevance ratio
ρ(q, d) = I(q ; d)relevant / tokens(d)

// ρ is structurally low for documents: purpose-built artifacts with broad scope.
// ρ → 0 as document length increases and query scope narrows.
Structural failure mode Even when a document contains the relevant concept, the token cost is paid at document scale, not concept scale. The coverage obtained does not scale with token expenditure. This is not a model quality problem — it is an architectural mismatch between document granularity and query granularity.
Concept Graph Retrieval: Efficiency by Construction

A concept node c is scoped to a single knowledge unit by design. Its information structure is narrow:

Concept node information structure
P(k | c) ≈ δk=c

// Approximately a point mass on the concept it encodes.
// I(q ; c) is high when c ∈ support(C(q)), near zero otherwise.

Define the coverage-per-token efficiency for a retrieved node n:

Definition — Retrieval Efficiency
Eff(q, n) = I(q ; n) / tokens(n)

// Mutual information gain per token consumed.

The architectural claim follows directly from the information structures of c and d:

Efficiency dominance
𝔼[Eff(q, c)] > 𝔼[Eff(q, d)]

// Because: I(q ; c) ≈ I(q ; d|relevant) — comparable signal
// But: tokens(c) << tokens(d) — far lower cost
// The gap widens as doc length ↑ and query scope ↓.
Amortization: Lower Bound on Cumulative Savings

Graph construction is a one-time cost per concept node. Query-time retrieval is a per-query cost. The amortization result shows the crossover at which accumulated efficiency savings recover construction cost.

Let a query q require coverage of k concept nodes from G. Define the per-query token costs:

Per-query token cost
Costdoc(q) = k · Td // k documents retrieved, each at full doc length
Costgraph(q) = k · Tc // k concept nodes retrieved, each scoped

// T_d = avg document tokens. T_c = avg concept node tokens. T_d >> T_c.

The per-query savings from graph retrieval versus document retrieval:

Per-query savings
Δ(q) = k · (Td − Tc)

Cumulative savings across N queries, against total construction cost for k concept nodes:

Cumulative savings vs. construction cost
Savings(N) = N · k · (Td − Tc) − k · Kc

// Construction cost: k nodes × K_c tokens each (paid once).
// Crossover at N* = K_c / (T_d − T_c).
Crossover query volume
N* = Kc / (Td − Tc)

// For N > N*: graph retrieval is strictly cheaper in cumulative terms.
// This is a LOWER BOUND — does not yet include dependency chain savings.
Lower bound interpretation This calculation assumes no dependency chain benefit — concept nodes are retrieved independently with no graph traversal. The actual savings are strictly higher once pre-encoded concept dependencies are accounted for (see open extension). The crossover N* is therefore an upper bound on when the architecture pays off.

Proof Sketch: Efficiency Dominance
(i)

A concept node c encodes a single knowledge unit by construction. Therefore tokens(c) = Tc where Tc is bounded and small — proportional to the depth of a single concept explanation.

(ii)

When c ∈ support(C(q)), I(q ; c) ≈ P(c | q) · H(c) — the full information content of concept c, weighted by its contribution to answering q. When c ∉ support(C(q)), I(q ; c) ≈ 0.

(iii)

A document d contains many knowledge units. Its token cost Td is paid in full regardless of which knowledge units are relevant to q. The relevant fraction ρ(q, d) = I(q ; d)relevant / tokens(d) satisfies ρ → 0 as document length grows and query scope narrows.

(iv)

In expectation across the query distribution, I(q ; c) ≈ I(q ; d)relevant when d contains c — both recover the same relevant signal. But tokens(c) ≪ tokens(d). Therefore:

𝔼[Eff(q, c)] = 𝔼[I(q ; c)] / Tc > 𝔼[I(q ; d)relevant] / Td = 𝔼[Eff(q, d)] □
Formal Rebuttals to Partial Solutions

Three partial solutions are commonly proposed as alternatives to the concept graph architecture. Each is absorbed into the existing formalism.

Objection 1 — Document chunking A chunk is a document with smaller tokens(n). Define Tch = average chunk size where Tc < Tch < Td. The efficiency ratio Eff(q, chunk) = I(q; chunk) / Tch improves over Eff(q, document). However, chunk boundaries are determined by token count or sentence windows — not by concept boundaries. Therefore P(k | chunk) remains diffuse: a chunk may contain partial concept explanation blended with irrelevant content. The dominance result Eff(q, c) > Eff(q, chunk) holds because tokens(c) is bounded by concept scope while I(q; chunk) < I(q; c) due to dilution. The efficiency gap narrows but does not close. Chunking is a document-side improvement that does not address the fundamental granularity mismatch.
Objection 2 — Solr / metadata index A metadata index resolves the navigation problem — get to the right document faster — but does not address coverage-per-token efficiency. Once the document is retrieved, the full token cost Td is still paid. Formally: the metadata index reduces retrieval latency but does not change I(q; d) or tokens(d). Eff(q, d) is unchanged. The index is a retrieval filter, not a knowledge representation.
Objection 3 — Enriched document metadata with concept/instance references If documents are tagged with canonical concept identifiers, Coverage(q, G) improves because documents can be retrieved by concept membership rather than keyword. This is the best document-centric approach. However, set membership {c₁, c₂, c₃} encodes presence but not structure. Two failures follow: (1) directionality is lost — prerequisite-of relationships between c₁ and c₂ are not representable in a tag set; the system cannot traverse dependency chains or rank retrieval order by conceptual dependency. (2) edge information content is lost — the typed predicate τ in an edge (c₁, τ, c₂) carries conditional information I(q; c₂ | c₁, τ) − I(q; c₂) that is strictly greater than zero for well-typed graphs. Tag-based approaches discard this lift entirely. Enriched metadata improves recall; it does not recover the information content of typed directed edges.
Extension: Instance Layer Efficiency

The concept layer addresses queries over knowledge units — what things are and how they relate. A second, separable layer addresses queries over instances — specific entities, their properties, and their relationships. The efficiency argument extends structurally intact.

Define an instance query as one that induces a distribution I(q) over specific entities and properties: not "what is NAV methodology" but "what was Carlyle CCP V's reported NAV at Q3 2024." Define an instance node as a typed entity with canonical identity and encoded properties.

Instance layer efficiency
Eff(q, instance node) = I(q ; instance node) / tokens(instance node)

// By the same argument: I(q ; instance node) ≈ I(q ; d)relevant when d contains the entity
// But tokens(instance node) << tokens(d) — a structured fact is far smaller than the document containing it
// Therefore Eff(q, instance node) > Eff(q, d) by the same mechanism as the concept layer

The entity resolution problem is the instance-layer analog of semantic bridging at the concept layer. "JPMorgan," "JPMorgan AM," "JPMorgan Asset Management," and a specific JPMorgan line of business are one entity or several, depending on canonical resolution. Without canonical instance nodes, queries over entity relationships return incomplete and inconsistent results — the same entity contributes to C(q) under multiple surface forms with no structural link.

Two-layer coverage The full coverage of query q decomposes into conceptual coverage Coverage(q, Gconcept) and instance coverage Coverage(q, Ginstance). Both are required for queries that have both conceptual dependencies (what is NAV methodology?) and instance dependencies (what was fund X's NAV?). The layers are separable — each delivers independent efficiency gains — and composable — together they cover the full query space without redundancy.

open: instance construction cost Instance graphs have higher construction cost per node (entity extraction, canonical resolution, deduplication, incremental refresh) and higher volatility than concept graphs. The amortization crossover N* differs. Full derivation is an open task.

dependency chains Pre-encoded concept dependencies reduce expected inference steps E[IG(q)] versus E[I0(q)]. Savings compound with chain depth.

instantiation depth H(c | d) — residual uncertainty about concept c after reading document d — is positive even when d contains c. A concept node drives H(c | node) → 0 by construction.

C(q) approximation The semantic bridging step is separable. Tractable estimation strategies in priority order: (1) LLM decomposition — day-one available, highest cost; (2) embedding similarity — cheap once infrastructure exists; (3) historical traversal patterns — near-zero cost as query logs accumulate. Cost of C(q) estimation decreases monotonically as the system matures.

What the system has to do

After retrieving the documents, the model must:

① Identify which parts of each document are relevant to the query — scanning content the system paid for but doesn't need.

② Reconstruct what NAV methodology means from passages spread across multiple documents, without a canonical definition anywhere.

③ Connect fair value methodology to NAV — a relationship that must be inferred rather than traversed.

④ Find the specific Q3 2024 figure — buried in an LP memo, not explicitly labeled.

⑤ Reconcile "Carlyle Group," "Carlyle CCP V," and "Carlyle Capital Partners" into the correct fund entity.

All of this happens at inference time, every time, at full token cost — with no guarantee of consistency across queries.

Three common improvements over Stage 0. Each moves the needle — and each leaves a residual gap that only typed graph structure closes.

① "We use chunked embeddings — that solves the token waste problem." ▶

Chunking improves the token cost denominator: a chunk of ~400 tokens costs far less than a 3,000-token document. Efficiency improves. But chunk boundaries are fixed by token count, not concept boundaries. The chunk containing "NAV methodology" may also contain half a paragraph about reporting timelines. P(k|chunk) is still diffuse.

More critically: two chunks discussing NAV methodology in different documents have no structural link. The system rediscovers that connection at inference time on every query. A concept node makes it once.

Moves the needle: ✓ Lower token cost per retrieval unit. Gap remains: chunk granularity ≠ concept granularity. The efficiency ratio improves but does not converge to the concept node case.
② "We have a Solr/metadata index — fast retrieval to the right document." ▶

A metadata index solves navigation: get to the right document faster. Typical fields: issuer, document type, date received, fund name. This reduces retrieval latency but does not change what happens once the document is retrieved. You still pay full synthesis cost on the document's content.

For this query: a Solr index gets you to the Carlyle LP memo faster. It does not tell you what NAV methodology means, how fair value relates to it, or what Carlyle's Q3 2024 figure was. You're still synthesizing from scratch.

Moves the needle: ✓ Faster navigation to relevant documents. Gap remains: retrieval filter is not knowledge representation. Synthesis cost is unchanged.
③ "What if we tag documents with canonical concept and entity references?" ▶

This is the strongest document-centric approach. Tagging documents with canonical concept identifiers {NAV methodology, fair value (ASC 820), vintage effects} enables concept-based retrieval without content scanning. Recall improves meaningfully.

But set membership tells you concepts are present — not how they relate. "NAV methodology" and "fair value estimation" are both in the tag set, but the system doesn't know that understanding fair value is a prerequisite for understanding how NAV is reported. That relationship is only in the graph.

Entity tags face the same problem: "Carlyle Group," "Carlyle CCP V," and "CCP V" may all be tagged but without canonical entity resolution, queries over entity relationships return inconsistent results.

Moves the needle: ✓ Better recall via concept-based retrieval. Gap remains: no directionality, no typed relationships, no dependency structure, no entity canonicalization. Graph information content is not recoverable from tags.

None of them encode concept dependencies, typed relationships, or canonical entity identity. That is what Stage 1 and Stage 2 provide.
