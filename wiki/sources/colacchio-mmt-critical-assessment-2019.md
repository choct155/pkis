## Summary

This 2019 Cambridge Centre for Economic and Public Policy working paper (CCEPP WP02-19) by Colacchio and Forges Davanzati offers a formal mathematical critique of Modern Money Theory (MMT) and proposes an extension of its policy agenda. MMT maintains that a sovereign state operating with a fiat currency can always finance public spending through monetisation, that taxation and bond issuance are not needed for deficit financing (only for inflation control), and that an Employer of Last Resort (ELR) programme can guarantee full employment without generating inflationary pressure.

The authors construct a Neo-Keynesian dynamic portfolio model in the tradition of Turnovsky (1977) to formalise MMT's logical framework. Their primary critique is that MMT's rejection of the Phillips curve leaves its inflation-control mechanism under-specified — without a Phillips curve there is no rigorous way to define a non-inflationary full-employment income level. They argue, against MMT's core claim, that both taxation and bond issues remain necessary instruments for correct implementation of economic policy: bond issuance facilitates portfolio rebalancing after monetary expansions, and taxes serve as instruments of real resource mobilisation and inflation dampening.

In section 4 the paper proposes that the ELR programme be expanded into a "State as Innovator of First Resort" (SIFR) framework: public employment should be directed not just at any work but specifically at high-skilled labour in R&D and technology sectors, where the "buffer stock" of unemployed workers in peripheral European regions is disproportionately composed of young, high-skilled individuals. This modification turns the ELR from a cyclical stabiliser into a structural driver of labour productivity growth.

## Key Knowledge Objects

- [[modern-money-theory]] (framework, high) — the heterodox macroeconomic school asserting that monetarily sovereign governments face no nominal financing constraint and can achieve full employment via ELR
- [[endogenous-money]] (concept, high) — the principle that money is created through the extension of credit by banks (or the state), not exogenously supplied; a shared foundation of MMT and the monetary circuit approach
- [[monetary-circuit]] (framework, moderate — could be concept) — the Italian/French post-Keynesian tradition (Graziani 2003) in which money is created ex nihilo by banks at the start of each production period and destroyed at the end; contrasts with MMT primarily on the role of the state
- [[state-innovator-of-first-resort]] (concept, moderate — could be framework) — Colacchio & Forges Davanzati's proposal to direct ELR employment toward R&D and innovation activities, converting buffer-stock employment into a structural productivity driver
- [[employer-of-last-resort]] (concept, high) — existing stub — the state provides employment at a fixed wage to all willing workers; Colacchio critiques its inflation-control mechanism and proposes an SIFR extension

## Key Extractions

1. **MMT's logical framework (p. 3):** "MMT describes the functioning of a pure credit economy which starts with an increase in public spending entirely financed via monetization on the part of the central bank. The central bank and the government are assumed to be a consolidated sector."

2. **Core critique — missing inflation equation (p. 6, fn. 9):** "This particular feature of MMT brings us back to Friedman's statement that Keynes's system is characterized by a 'missing equation', that is, the equation that relates the real with the monetary part of the economic system."

3. **Critique of bond-irrelevance claim (p. 11):** Against MMT's claim that bond issues are irrelevant to deficit financing, the authors show that "the presence of an adequate stock of public bonds can make the process of wealth reallocation easier after some kind of 'perturbation'."

4. **Monetary circuit vs. MMT distinction (p. 2):** "While in the first case [monetary circuit], money creation can be generated via transactions inside the banking sector … in the second case [MMT], credit money is generated by public spending on the condition that the central bank and the government act as a consolidated sector."

5. **Turnovsky stability result (p. 6):** A pure money-financing policy (fixed real bond stock) is "more stable than the other two [policies], because it tends to be the most inflationary policy, generating the greatest inflation tax revenues and thereby providing the strongest stabilizing influence" — citing Turnovsky (1977, p. 53). The paper uses this result to motivate why taxes and bonds remain necessary.

6. **SIFR proposal (Section 4):** ELR employment should be directed at R&D and public innovation activities because in peripheral European regions "the 'buffer stock' is mainly populated by young high-skilled individuals. Public spending designed to increase the level of employment in the public sector and for R&D activities not only produces an increase in employment but also an increase in labour productivity and the growth rate."

## Connection Candidates

- [[functional-finance]] — grounds: MMT's policy prescriptions rest on Abba Lerner's functional finance principle that fiscal policy should be judged by its economic effects rather than any "sound finance" norm
- [[quantity-theory-of-money]] — contrasts-with: QTM asserts money supply excess drives inflation; MMT denies this for demand-deficit conditions and asserts that ELR anchors prices through a buffer-stock mechanism rather than through monetary quantity limits
- [[state-theory-of-money]] — prerequisite-of: the consolidated-sector assumption in this model traces directly to Knapp's chartalist claim that the state defines money via tax obligations
- [[employer-of-last-resort]] — extends: SIFR extends ELR by directing state employment specifically toward high-skill R&D roles rather than any willing-worker buffer stock
