## Summary

Gavetti, Greve, Levinthal, and Ocasio (2012) provide a retrospective and prospective assessment of Cyert and March's (1963) "A Behavioral Theory of the Firm" (BTF), one of the three Carnegie School cornerstones (along with Simon 1947 and March-Simon 1958) of organization theory. The paper documents the BTF's enormous influence across organizational theory, strategic management, and neighboring fields, while identifying where its original agenda has been advanced, where it has been neglected, and where new developments in neighboring fields should be incorporated.

The paper identifies four foundational cognitive concepts from Cyert and March: (1) **satisficing** — agents choose the first satisfactory alternative rather than maximizing; (2) **problemistic search** — search is triggered by performance below aspiration levels, focused near current symptoms and solutions; (3) **standard operating procedures and rules** — organizations use rule-based behavior to avoid forecasting distant futures; and (4) **aspiration levels** — threshold performance benchmarks that determine what counts as satisfactory, themselves adapted to past performance. Four relational mechanisms translate these cognitive foundations into organizational behavior: quasi-resolution of conflict, uncertainty avoidance, problemistic search, and organizational learning.

The paper then surveys research developments across six topics: (1) cognition and cognitive representations (from standard operating procedures to routines; from expectations to anticipatory cognitive representations); (2) performance feedback research (how performance relative to aspiration levels drives organizational change); (3) politics and power in organizations; (4) attention-based view of the firm (Ocasio's theory that what firms do depends on how they focus managerial attention); (5) organizational learning; and (6) adaptation and the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. The paper concludes that the BTF's agenda benefits from both its adherents and from integration with related research traditions — including evolutionary economics (Nelson-Winter), cognitive science, and complexity theory.

## Key Knowledge Objects

- [[satisficing]] (concept, high) — choosing the first alternative that meets an aspiration level rather than searching for the optimal alternative
- [[problemistic-search]] (concept, high) — search triggered by performance falling below aspiration levels; focused near the current problem and current solutions
- [[aspiration-levels]] (concept, high) — performance thresholds that determine satisfactory outcomes; adapt toward historically experienced performance
- [[organizational-learning]] (concept, high) — encoding inferences from experience into routines that guide future behavior; Levitt and March (1988) formulation
- [[exploration-exploitation]] (concept, high) — March (1991) tradeoff between exploring new options and exploiting known competencies; fundamental tension in organizational adaptation
- [[attention-based-view]] (framework, high) — Ocasio's theory that firm behavior is determined by the distribution of attention among organizational actors; what firms notice and focus on governs strategic action
- [[routines]] (concept, high) — organizational analogs to individual skills (Nelson-Winter); semi-automatic, distributed procedural memories encoding collective competencies
- behavioral-theory-of-the-firm (low — concept or framework?) — the Carnegie School synthesis as a whole; straddles between a theoretical framework and a broad research program

## Key Extractions

1. The BTF's agenda: "A general theory of how fundamental decisions such as price and output decisions happen in organizations was missing. The call was then for research on 'the fundamental decisions of the firm... and an explicit emphasis on the actual process of decision making as its basic research commitment.'" (Cyert & March 1963, p. 19, quoted on p. 3)

2. Satisficing: "Individuals do not maximize. They satisfice. Roughly speaking, this means that they choose the first alternative they expect to be satisfactory. What is deemed 'satisfactory' depends on an aspiration level, which in turn depends on a series of factors, most notably the history of prior performance." (p. 4–5)

3. Problemistic search: "Failures to achieve a satisfactory outcome trigger search processes... Search processes stop when an alternative is expected to be satisfactory... Rule-based behavior avoids foresight by exploiting the wisdom of experience." (p. 5–6)

4. Relational concepts: The four relational concepts — quasi-resolution of conflict, uncertainty avoidance, problemistic search, and organizational learning — "lead to the theoretical synthesis of a Behavioral Theory of the Firm" as a more behaviorally realistic alternative to the optimizing firm of standard economics. (p. 6)

5. Anticipatory cognitive representations: Gavetti and Levinthal (2000) showed that decision makers use "simplified cognitive representations of their decision environment to anticipate, in approximate terms, the long-term implications of alternative courses of action" — enriching the BTF's original treatment of purely reactive, local search.

6. Organizational routines as distributed procedural memory: Cohen and Bacdayan (1994) experiments show that routines are stored as distributed procedural memories; their "misfiring when the problem suddenly changes" explains vulnerability of routinized organizations to environmental discontinuities.
