## Summary

This IEA Current Controversies paper (No. 77, April 2021) by Juan Castañeda — Director of the Institute of International Monetary Research and a monetarist in the Friedman/Congdon tradition — provides a critical assessment of Modern Monetary Theory from the perspective of quantity-theory monetarism. The paper was prompted by the extraordinary expansion of central bank balance sheets during Covid-19 (US M3 up 22%, UK M4 up 14% in 2020) and asks whether this partial monetisation of deficits vindicates MMT or should instead be halted.

Castañeda's critique has three major components. First, he argues that MMT correctly identifies the mechanics of a fiat monetary system (governments can always create money) but ignores the macroeconomic consequences: excess money supply relative to goods and services will generate inflation with a 3–6 quarter lag, as demonstrated historically. The quantity theory's equation of exchange (MV = PT), applied empirically to broad money, provides this link. Second, Castañeda runs counterfactual simulations for the UK and US showing that "partial MMT" (monetising the deficit) would have produced money growth of ~37% in the US and ~25% in the UK in 2020, and "full MMT" (monetising all government spending) would have produced 53% and 48% respectively — rates historically incompatible with price stability. Third, he argues that full adoption of MMT would produce "fiscal dominance," in which the central bank loses operational independence and accommodates all Treasury financing needs, eliminating any anti-inflationary anchor.

Castañeda also challenges two specific MMT claims: that monetary sovereignty guarantees no default risk (he counters with Venezuela, Zimbabwe, Argentina as cases where fiat issuance collapsed currency value), and that bank-created money is irrelevant (he argues the bulk of payments in modern economies is broad bank money, not monetary base, so the money multiplier matters even if unstable).

The paper explicitly targets Wray, Mitchell, Watts (*Macroeconomics*, 2019), Mosler (2020), and Kelton (*The Deficit Myth*, 2020) as the primary MMT sources under critique.

## Key Extractions

1. Castañeda distinguishes "partial MMT" (central bank monetises the deficit) from "full MMT" (central bank monetises all government spending) and computes the implied broad money growth for both scenarios in the UK and US for 2020–2022.

2. "For an advanced economy to maintain a non-inflationary rate of growth, the amount of money should grow in the region of 5–6 per cent per annum." (Friedman's K% rule as Castañeda's benchmark — compatible with ~2% real growth, ~2% inflation, ~1% velocity decline.)

3. "The demand for money is not infinite and remains quite stable over the medium to the long term. If the supply of money systematically exceeds the production of goods and services in the economy, first asset prices, but also eventually CPI prices, will rise accordingly." (The portfolio rebalancing mechanism through which monetary excess propagates to prices.)

4. "MMT-ers are right that a fiat currency is not backed by any asset... But being fully fiat is precisely what makes its acceptance completely dependent on the ability of the currency to be used as a means of exchange in ordinary transactions." (The reputational constraint on monetary sovereignty.)

5. Castañeda notes that the US monetary base expanded 80% in 2009 while broad money (M3) actually fell in 2008–2009 — demonstrating the instability of the money multiplier in crises and why central bank balance sheet expansion alone is insufficient evidence for inflation.

6. The paper acknowledges MMT's correct identification of endogenous money creation by banks (citing Mitchell et al. 2019: 154–157) but argues MMT fails to integrate this into its inflation analysis.

7. "In an MMT-dominated world, the central bank's only role would be to accommodate fully the financial needs of the Treasury, determined by the government's own economic policies and political goals — leading to a 'fiscal dominance' scenario." (Definition of fiscal dominance in the MMT context.)
