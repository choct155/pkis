## Summary

Tim Congdon's 2024 monograph (Institute of Economic Affairs) restates and defends the quantity theory of money, motivated by the inflation episode of 2021–2023 that mainstream economists failed to forecast. Congdon argues that excessive growth in broad money — driven by quantitative easing (central bank asset purchases from non-banks) during Covid — was the proximate cause of the 2022 inflation surge peaking near 10% in the UK and US, vindicating Milton Friedman's dictum that "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."

The restatement differs from Friedman's Chicago-School monetarism in several important respects. Congdon insists on a broadly defined money aggregate (bank deposits, not just monetary base or narrow money), argues that the monetary base multiplier is unreliable, and criticises Friedman's vacillations over which money aggregate matters. The core theoretical contribution is an explicit account of the transmission mechanism: monetary equilibrium is established when non-bank private sector agents' demand to hold broad money equals the quantity created by the banking system. When money shocks shatter equilibrium, the adjustment operates primarily through variable-income assets — equities and real estate — rather than through bond yields as in the Keynesian IS-LM framework or New Keynesian three-equation models.

Congdon's "proportionality postulate" asserts that changes in broad money and nominal national income are equi-proportionate in equilibrium; in practice, "monetary disequilibrium" often prevails but velocity is stable enough over multi-year windows that broad money growth predicts nominal GDP growth. Chapters 8 and 9 apply the framework to the US and UK Covid periods, demonstrating that the money explosion of spring–summer 2020 (US M3 growth reaching 25% annualised in June 2020) predictably produced the inflation of 2022. Chapter 10 delineates the differences from Friedman; chapter 11 defends the framework as the only one that correctly predicted the post-Covid inflation.

## Key Knowledge Objects

- [[quantity-theory-of-money]] (framework, high) — the proposition that changes in the money supply determine the price level and nominal income in equilibrium; formalised as MV = PT
- [[monetary-transmission-mechanism]] (concept, high) — the channels through which changes in money supply affect real and nominal economic outcomes, emphasised here as operating via variable-income asset prices
- [[monetary-equilibrium]] (concept, high) — the state in which non-bank private sector agents' desired money holdings equal the quantity of money supplied by the banking system
- [[proportionality-postulate]] (principle, high) — Congdon's claim that in equilibrium, changes in broad money and nominal national income are equiproportionate
- [[broad-money-monetarism]] (framework, moderate — could be concept) — the position that a broadly defined money aggregate (bank deposits inclusive) rather than monetary base or narrow money is the relevant causal variable for inflation
- [[financialisation]] (concept, moderate — could be principle) — the long-run tendency for financial transactions to grow faster than income, requiring a qualification to the proportionality postulate
- [[equation-of-exchange]] (result, high) — the Fisher identity MV = PT relating money quantity, velocity, price level, and transactions volume
- endogenous-money-creation (low — concept or result?) — the claim that money is created by bank credit extension to states and non-bank private sectors, not as a simple multiple of central bank liabilities

## Key Extractions

1. **Core proposition (p. 6):** "Monetary equilibrium is established when non-bank private sector agents' demand to hold all money balances (i.e. broad money) is equal to the quantity of money created by the banking system and its customers."

2. **Proportionality postulate (p. 6):** "If certain assumptions are met, changes in the quantity of money and nominal national income are equi-proportionate in equilibrium ('the proportionality postulate'). In practice, the required assumptions are rarely met in full and 'monetary disequilibrium' often prevails."

3. **Variable-income asset channel (p. 6):** "Changes in the value of variable-income assets (equities, real estate) — often due to changes in the quantity of money — are a central feature of the transmission mechanism. The Keynesian claim that the transmission mechanism involves only changes in the value of bonds (i.e. in 'the rate of interest'), as in 'the IS function' of the textbooks, is a serious misunderstanding."

4. **Critique of New Keynesianism (Summary):** "Interest-rate-only macroeconomics — particularly as exemplified in the three-equation version of New Keynesianism — has ostracised money from central bank research in the twenty-first century. This kind of money-less macroeconomics is the key intellectual mistake behind the 'dismal performance' and 'collective failure'."

5. **Covid prediction (Introduction):** On 23 April 2020, Congdon published "Get ready for the return of inflation" in the Wall Street Journal; peak US M3 growth came in June 2020 at over 25%. Consumer prices rose 7% in the year to December 2021.

6. **Broad money vs. base money (Summary):** "This study emphasises the importance of a broadly defined money aggregate in the determination of nominal national income and wealth. It differs from Chicago School monetarism by denying that the monetary base and narrow money play a major causative role in a modern economy."

7. **Financialisation qualification (Summary):** "The proportionality postulate … needs to be qualified by 'financialisation'. This is the tendency for financial transactions (and hence the need for money) to grow faster than income as economies progress."
