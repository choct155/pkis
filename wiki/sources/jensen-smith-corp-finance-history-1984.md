## Summary

Jensen and Smith's 1984 overview (introductory chapter to their edited volume *The Modern Theory of Corporate Finance*) traces the transformation of corporate finance from ad hoc, prescriptive description before 1950 to a rigorous, positive-theory-driven field by the early 1980s. The pre-1950 state of the field (exemplified by Dewing's textbook) was characterized by institutional detail and logical inconsistency — normative prescriptions about optimal investment, financing, and dividend policies without a scientific basis in equilibrium theory.

The paper identifies five foundational "building blocks" developed between 1950 and 1980:

1. **Efficient Market Theory** (Samuelson, Mandelbrot, Fama): unexpected price changes are independent random drawings in competitive markets; technical trading cannot earn above-normal returns; market prices reflect all available information.

2. **Portfolio Theory** (Markowitz): optimal portfolios maximize expected return for given variance; diversification has formal content; firms should evaluate projects like investors evaluate securities (portfolio diversification creates no value by itself).

3. **Capital Asset Pricing Theory** (Treynor, Sharpe, Lintner): equilibrium expected return = RF + β × (E[RM] − RF); only systematic risk (covariance with market) is priced; idiosyncratic risk is diversified away. This defines the opportunity cost of capital.

4. **Option Pricing Theory** (Black-Scholes): equity of a levered firm is a call option on total assets with exercise price equal to face value of debt; this framework values contingent claims generally.

5. **Agency Theory** (Jensen-Meckling, Fama, Jensen): agency costs arise from conflicts between principals and agents; these are borne by the parties to the contract; parties will innovate to reduce them. The "market for corporate control" (hostile takeovers) disciplines management.

The paper then surveys applications to capital budgeting (NPV using CAPM-derived discount rates), capital structure (trade-off theory, signaling, contracting), and dividend policy (clientele effects, signaling, irrelevance under MM).

## Key Extractions

1. Pre-1950 state: "Corporate financial theory prior to the 1950s was riddled with logical inconsistencies and was almost totally prescriptive … little consideration was given to the effect on these policies of individual incentives, or to the nature of equilibrium in financial markets."

2. EMH implication for corporate finance: "Managers should maximize the current market value of the firm … there is no benefit to manipulating earnings per share … concern about dilution … is eliminated … security returns are meaningful measures of firm performance."

3. CAPM: "Asset-pricing theory defines the opportunity cost of capital for the firm's capital budgeting decisions." E(R_j) = RF + [E(RM) − RF] × β_j.

4. Agency theory (Jensen-Meckling, 1976): "Agency costs [are] the sum of the costs of structuring contracts (formal and informal): monitoring expenditures by the principal, bonding expenditures by the agent, and the residual loss." Parties rationally anticipate these costs, so they are borne by the parties at contract formation.

5. Market for corporate control: "Successful tender offers produce approximately 30 percent abnormal stock price performance in [target] firms." This represents value creation from improved management.

6. Shift in research paradigm: The 1950s transformation was "a change in the focus of the literature from normative questions such as 'What should investment, financing, or dividend policies be?' to positive theories addressing questions such as 'What are the effects of alternative investment, financing, or dividend policies on the value of the firm?'"
