## Summary

Fama's 2013 paper (Review of Asset Pricing Studies, Vol. 3, pp. 180–199), updated in October 2022, examines empirically whether the Federal Reserve controls interest rates and, if so, to what extent. The drive file is the 2022 update (Chicago Booth Paper No. 12-23), a 6-page note that summarises and extends the original findings through the 2022 tightening cycle.

The original paper's central finding is confirmed: the Fed definitively controls the Federal Funds rate (the overnight interbank borrowing rate), but its influence attenuates rapidly along the term structure. The 5-year and 10-year Treasury rates show little systematic relation to FF target rate changes; they are more plausibly driven by inflation expectations, risk premia, and global demand for safe assets — factors beyond the Fed's direct control.

The paper raises the more fundamental question of causality at the short end: do market rates forecast Fed actions (forward-looking markets adjusting to predictable policy), or does the Fed accommodate market rates (passive central bank)? The 3-month T-bill consistently leads FF target changes by several months, but this is equally consistent with both interpretations and Fama concludes the question cannot be resolved empirically.

A significant contribution of the 2022 update is the analysis of how QE restructured the relationship between the Fed and interest rates. Under pre-QE arrangements, the Fed could control the price level via the monetary base (reserves and currency, both with positive opportunity costs). Under QE, reserves pay interest equal to the FF target, eliminating their opportunity cost and making them indistinguishable from short-term government debt. This means the Fed can no longer control the price level through reserve management — inflation becomes "out of Fed control." Fama reads the Fed's post-2010 inflation language ("keeping an eye on it") as a quiet acknowledgment of this constraint.

The 2022 update notes that the Fed's response to ~8% inflation — raising FF in steps to lean against aggregate demand — is a "new approach to an inflation problem" whose effectiveness is uncertain, given that FF's influence on rates above very short maturities is weak and risk premia are beyond the Fed's control.

## Key Knowledge Objects

- [[federal-funds-rate-targeting]] (concept, high) — the Fed's operational procedure of setting a target for the overnight interbank lending rate and adjusting the interest rate paid on reserves to maintain it
- [[term-structure-of-interest-rates]] (concept, high) — the relationship between interest rates and time to maturity; Fama provides evidence that the Fed's influence attenuates rapidly beyond the overnight segment
- [[interest-rate-on-reserves]] (concept, high) — the rate the Fed pays on bank reserves held at the Fed; under QE this becomes the primary mechanism for inducing banks to hold excess reserves and equals the FF target
- [[quantitative-easing]] (concept, moderate — could be framework) — the Fed program of issuing reserves to purchase long-term bonds; Fama argues QE restructured the entire relationship between the Fed, reserves, and price-level control

## Key Extractions

1. "The paper finds that the Fed controls the Federal Funds (FF) rate (the overnight rate on interbank borrowing of reserves). Other short-term rates are related to FF, but the relations between FF and other rates weaken quickly for longer maturities." (Core finding of the 2013 paper, confirmed out-of-sample through 2022.)

2. "If the Fed's effects on interest rates are limited to the very short end of the term structure, the effects of its actions on real activity are likely to be quite limited. For example, it seems unlikely that firms change their costs of capital depending on what the Fed does with the FF rate." (The implication for monetary policy effectiveness at affecting investment.)

3. "Once they pay interest at or above market rates, reserves are just short-term debt." (The key structural change introduced by QE: reserves lose their distinctiveness as high-powered money.)

4. "The Fed could still control the price level if it could control the supply of currency. But by law the Fed must satisfy bank demands to swap currency for reserves and vice versa, so the Fed can't control the supply of currency." (Why QE eliminates the Fed's pre-existing price level control mechanism.)

5. "The Fed losing control of the price level doesn't mean high inflation. It just means inflation is what it is, that is, out of Fed control." (Fama's carefully qualified conclusion about the consequences of the QE regime.)

6. Fama's explanation for persistently low short rates despite massive QE (2009–2022): strong global demand for riskless short-term debt driven by elevated risk aversion, making the low rates demand-driven rather than supply-driven. This is a testable empirical claim that has since been partially challenged by the 2022 inflation surge.

## Connection Candidates

- [[monetary-transmission-mechanism]] — contrasts-with: Fama's finding that FF effects attenuate rapidly along the term structure challenges the standard Keynesian transmission story (Fed → short rates → long rates → investment); supports Congdon's portfolio-rebalancing channel as an alternative
- [[quantity-theory-of-money]] — contrasts-with: the QTM (castaneda-mmt-critique-2021, congdon-quantity-theory-2024) predicts broad money growth drives prices; Fama's paper is skeptical that the Fed controls even the price level mechanism, suggesting an important complementarity between these critiques
- [[chartalism]] (wray-modern-money-1998) — contrasts-with: Wray argues bond sales are interest-rate management tools, not financing tools; Fama independently supports this by showing FF control is the Fed's primary lever — but Fama's QE analysis complicates Wray's story by showing QE altered the mechanics
- [[efficient-market-hypothesis]] — grounds: the leading of FF changes by T-bill rates is consistent with forward-looking markets pricing predictable Fed actions, which is the EMH interpretation Fama favors

## Awaiting Classification

- **quantitative-easing** — candidate types: concept or framework
  - Case for concept: QE has a precise operational definition (reserves issued to purchase longer-maturity assets) and clear boundaries
  - Case for framework: in Fama's analysis, QE is presented as a systematic policy regime that fundamentally restructured the relationship between reserves, interest rates, and inflation control — it organizes a set of mechanisms rather than naming a single object
  - What makes this hard: QE is at once a specific policy tool (concept) and a paradigm shift in central banking that reorganized the entire system (framework)
