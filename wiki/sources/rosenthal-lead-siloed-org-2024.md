## Summary

Rosenthal and Rosen (HBR, 2024) argue that the dominant model of executive development — building vertical leadership skill within a function — has become insufficient given the degree to which real work now crosses organizational boundaries. Drawing on CEO interviews and a study of dozens of senior leaders nominated as high performers in cross-unit collaboration, the authors identify a syndrome they call "siloed organizations" and the corrective capability they term "lateral agility": the ability to move across organizational functions quickly while maintaining balance and relationships.

The article identifies three trait clusters separating high-performing cross-functional leaders from peers. First, an "expanded mindset" characterized by enterprise-first thinking (subordinating functional objectives to organizational objectives) and epistemic humility about where expertise resides. Second, "connective skills" — curiosity and empathy deployed to build understanding of colleagues' constraints and motivations, combined with next-level negotiation practices that create mutual-gain solutions rather than zero-sum tradeoffs. Third, "innovative practices" including the "make purple" heuristic (bringing a sketch rather than a finished plan to collaborative conversations, enabling co-creation), and proactive relationship investment with the stakeholders most critical to cross-unit execution.

The article frames lateral agility as an organizational capability that requires intentional development, not merely individual disposition. It recommends that organizations re-examine incentive structures that inadvertently reward functional performance at the expense of cross-enterprise cooperation, and proposes three self-assessment practices for individual leaders: stakeholder mapping by strategic importance versus interaction frequency, time allocation auditing (leading across vs. leading own team), and deliberate perspective-taking with peers outside one's function.

## Key Knowledge Objects

- [[lateral-agility]] (concept, high) — the leadership capacity to move across organizational functions effectively without formal authority, analogous to physical lateral movement that requires maintaining balance
- [[cross-functional-leadership]] (concept, high) — the practice of driving outcomes by coordinating with peers who have different agendas, motivations, and priorities without hierarchical authority
- [[organizational-silos]] (concept, high) — structural barriers arising from functional specialization that impede information flow, collaborative decision-making, and coordinated execution across units
- [[enterprise-first-mindset]] (principle, high) — the normative orientation of prioritizing enterprise objectives over functional, team, or personal goals when making cross-boundary decisions
- [[stakeholder-mapping]] (technique, high) — a self-assessment practice of inventorying key stakeholders by strategic importance versus frequency of interaction to identify relationship investment gaps
- [[paradoxical-negotiation-questions]] (technique, moderate — could be principle) — a rhetorical and negotiation technique using questions framed to acknowledge both parties' objectives simultaneously, forcing expansive rather than zero-sum thinking

## Key Extractions

1. "Lateral agility" is defined as "the ability to move side to side quickly while maintaining their balance" — borrowed from physical agility to describe the leadership capacity for cross-functional navigation without loss of focus on one's own unit's objectives.

2. "Enterprise-first" framing: high-performing cross-functional leaders consistently phrase decisions as "What do the organization or our customers need from our work together?" rather than what their own function needs.

3. On expertise humility: "My job is to synthesize the wisdom of all these experts in their various roles and weave it all together." One leader adds: "Sometimes followership is the greatest act of leadership."

4. The "make purple" heuristic: "I bring red, you bring blue, and together we create something purple — something different and better than either of us would have created alone." The practice of bringing a "sketch" (not a blank page, not a finished plan) enables co-creation.

5. Paradoxical negotiation framing: "How can you and I find a solution that enables me to achieve what I'm trying to do, while also enabling your team to meet its objectives?" — forces expansive thinking and avoids either/or discussions.

6. CEO research finding: every CEO interviewed in the study cited the inability to get executive teams to collaborate cross-functionally as a top frustration. "I'm the only one who thinks about the entire company."

7. Recommended self-assessment: map top 5–8 stakeholders against (a) strategic importance to you and (b) frequency of interaction. Mismatches between importance and interaction frequency signal misallocated relationship investment.
