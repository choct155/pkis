## Summary

Coase's 1937 paper asks a deceptively simple question: if the price mechanism efficiently coordinates production across an entire economy, why do firms exist at all? The answer, which launched the modern theory of the firm, is that using the price mechanism is itself costly. Discovering relevant prices, negotiating separate contracts for each exchange transaction, and managing uncertainty all impose costs on market participants. Firms arise precisely because they can economize on these "marketing" or transaction costs: replacing a series of spot-market contracts with a single, authority-based employment relation in which a worker agrees to follow an entrepreneur's direction within specified limits.

Coase identifies three determinants of firm size: (1) the increasing marginal cost of organizing additional transactions internally — as a firm grows, the entrepreneur may misallocate resources or the administrative costs may rise; (2) the rising supply price of factors as firm scale increases; (3) the spatial and product-type distribution of transactions, which raises the cost of internal organization. The firm expands until the cost of organizing one more transaction internally equals the cost of either market exchange or organizing via a different entrepreneur. This "marginalist" logic makes firm size analytically tractable.

The paper also clarifies the concepts of combination (bringing together transactions previously organized by multiple entrepreneurs) and integration (replacing market transactions between entrepreneurs with internal organization). It engages critiques from Knight (who attributed firm emergence to uncertainty and residual risk bearing) and other contemporaries, arguing that while uncertainty matters, the key explanatory variable is the cost of contracting — not the nature of the residual claim.

Coase's paper is foundational to transaction cost economics, the property rights theory of the firm, and modern contract theory. It anticipates Williamson's later treatment of bounded rationality and asset specificity, and grounds the Jensen-Meckling and Fama nexus-of-contracts perspectives.

## Key Extractions

1. "The main reason why it is profitable to establish a firm would seem to be that there is a cost of using the price mechanism." (p. 390)

2. Firm defined: "A firm, therefore, consists of the system of relationships which comes into existence when the direction of resources is dependent on an entrepreneur." The distinguishing characteristic is that a factor agrees "to obey the directions of an entrepreneur within certain limits."

3. Firm size determinant: "A firm will tend to expand until the costs of organising an extra transaction within the firm become equal to the costs of carrying out the same transaction by means of an exchange on the open market or the costs of organising in another firm."

4. Firm grows larger as: (a) costs of organizing rise more slowly with scale; (b) the entrepreneur makes fewer mistakes; (c) supply price of factors does not rise quickly with size.

5. On Knight's uncertainty argument: Coase argues that uncertainty is not sufficient to explain firm emergence — a system of advice-selling or pure contracting could manage uncertainty without establishing authoritative direction. The key is that direction economizes on *contracting* costs.

6. Legal alignment: The legal concept of "employer and employee" (master's right to control servant's work) corresponds to Coase's economic concept of the firm, validating the definition's realism.
