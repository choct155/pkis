## Summary

Fama (1980) examines commercial banking from the perspective of the theory of finance, arguing that in a competitive, unregulated banking system the portfolio management activities of banks — issuing deposits and purchasing securities — are pure financing decisions covered by the Modigliani-Miller theorem. Because competitive banks simply respond to the tastes and opportunities of demanders and suppliers of portfolio assets, the banking sector is passive with respect to the determination of prices and real activity. No regulation of bank deposit creation or asset purchasing is required to obtain a stable general equilibrium.

The paper draws a sharp conceptual distinction between two types of transactions systems: currency (a physical medium of exchange) and an accounting system of exchange (bookkeeping debits and credits). In an unregulated banking system, deposits need not be homogeneous and cannot serve as a natural numeraire. The analysis of numeraire determinacy therefore does not require controlling the banking system per se; it can be achieved by controlling the supply of a pure nominal commodity such as currency. However, in practice, reserve requirements forcibly link the holding of non-interest-bearing central bank reserves to the provision of accounting transactions services, thereby creating a real demand for nominal units of account and making banks involuntary participants in defining the price level. The paper concludes with an allegorical "parable" about a society that replaces a real-goods numeraire with a pure nominal unit, illustrating that reserve requirements are ultimately a taxing mechanism rather than a stability necessity.

## Key Extractions

1. "When banking is competitive, the portfolio management activities of banks are the type of pure financing decisions covered by the Modigliani-Miller (1958) theorem. From this result we can infer that there is no need to control either the deposit creation or the security purchasing activities of banks for the purpose of obtaining a stable general equilibrium with respect to prices and real activity." (p. 40)

2. The accounting system of exchange operates without a physical medium — debits and credits generate sales and purchases of securities from portfolios, and the numeraire "never appears physically in the process of exchange." (p. 42)

3. "In a system where deposits can take on the characteristics of any form of invested wealth, deposits are a means of payment only in the sense that all forms of wealth are a means of payment, and the banking system is best understood without the mischief introduced by the concept of money." (p. 44)

4. "Controlling the supply of currency alone is sufficient to render the price level (the real value of the unit of account) determinate" — government need not also control bank reserves or deposits. (p. 54, paraphrasing Patinkin)

5. "The reserve requirement is simply a tax on deposit returns" that creates demand for non-interest-bearing central bank reserves by linking them to a service (accounting exchange) that is genuinely valued. (p. 52)

## Awaiting Classification

- **accounting-system-of-exchange** — candidate types: concept or framework
  - Case for concept: It is an idea with a boundary — a definition of what bank transactions really are (bookkeeping entries, not physical media)
  - Case for framework: It organizes how to think about the entire banking-transactions system and has component mechanisms (debits, credits, portfolio purchases)
  - What makes this hard: Fama uses it as a definitional concept but it carries enough structure that it could anchor a mini-framework for thinking about monetary systems

- **numeraire-determination** — candidate types: concept or result
  - Case for concept: It is a well-defined problem (how does a unit of account become a real economic good?) with a stable definition
  - Case for result: Fama demonstrates (following Patinkin) that controlling currency supply alone is sufficient — this is a theoretical finding
  - What makes this hard: The paper presents both the problem and the answer; the object straddles "what is this?" and "what do we know for certain?"
