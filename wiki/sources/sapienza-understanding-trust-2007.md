## Summary

Sapienza, Toldra, and Zingales (2007 SSRN working paper) resolve a longstanding empirical puzzle in the trust literature: Glaeser et al. (2000) found that the World Values Survey (WVS) trust question correlates with receiver (not sender) behavior in the standard trust game, concluding it measures trustworthiness rather than trust; Fehr et al. (2003) found the opposite using a large German household sample. The paper argues both findings can be reconciled by recognizing that trust has two separable components: (1) a belief-based component — the expectation that others will behave trustworthily — and (2) a preference-based component — individual dispositions like risk aversion, reciprocity, and altruism that also shape trusting behavior.

The empirical design uses the Templeton Chicago Longitudinal MBA Sample (N ≈ 502 consenting MBA students). Subjects played a modified trust game in which the sender first sends money (tripled), then elicits beliefs about how much the receiver would return under each possible sending amount (incentivized using a 10% accuracy bonus), and finally plays as a receiver using the strategy method. This allows direct separation of beliefs from preferences in the sender's behavior.

Key findings: (1) The sender's expected-trustworthiness beliefs are highly correlated with the WVS question and with actual amount sent. (2) The WVS question captures the belief-based component of trust, not the preference component. (3) In homogeneous populations (Chicago MBAs, Harvard undergraduates), agents infer others' trustworthiness by introspecting their own behavior, generating a spurious correlation between WVS-measured trust and measured trustworthiness — explaining Glaeser et al.'s result. (4) In heterogeneous populations (German households), this inference breaks down — explaining Fehr et al.'s result. The paper thus offers a structural decomposition of trust with direct implications for survey measure validity.

## Key Knowledge Objects

- [[trust-decomposition]] (concept, high) — the partition of trust into belief-based (expectations about others) and preference-based (risk aversion, reciprocity, altruism) components
- [[belief-based-trust]] (concept, high) — the expectational component of trust: what fraction of one's trusting behavior reflects beliefs about the receiver's trustworthiness
- [[preference-based-trust]] (concept, moderate — could be concept or principle) — the dispositional component of trust: how risk aversion, reciprocity, and altruism shape trusting behavior independent of beliefs
- [[trust-game-methodology]] (technique, high) — the Berg-Dickhaut-McCabe (1995) experimental paradigm measuring trust (amount sent) and trustworthiness (fraction returned), with extensions for belief elicitation

## Key Extractions

1. "Trust has two components: a belief-based one and a preference based one. While the sender behavior's reflects both, we show that WVS-like measures capture mostly the belief-based component, while questions on past trusting behavior are better at capturing the preference component of trust." (Abstract)
2. Experimental design: first movers endowed with $50, can send multiples of $5; amount sent tripled; strategy-method belief elicitation incentivized at $10 per answer within 10% of actual return.
3. Subjects were overly optimistic: expected proportion returned was consistently 5 percentage points higher than actual proportion returned, but they correctly anticipated the slope of reciprocity.
4. WVS trust question correlates with sender's expectations of receiver trustworthiness when the stakes are economically significant (larger amounts sent); for small stakes, expectations reflect anticipated retaliation rather than general trust.
5. "In highly homogenous populations (Chicago MBAs, Harvard undergraduates) players tend to form their expectations by introspecting their own behavior. This inference is not true in very heterogeneous populations." — explains the Glaeser/Fehr divergence.
6. Risk aversion measurement uses Holt-Laury (2002) lottery protocol to separate altruism/reciprocity from risk aversion in the trust-game behavior.
