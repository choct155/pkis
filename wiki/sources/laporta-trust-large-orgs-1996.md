## Summary

La Porta, Lopez-de-Silanes, Shleifer, and Vishny (NBER Working Paper 5864, 1996) extend earlier theoretical work by Putnam (1993), Coleman (1990), and Fukuyama (1995) by empirically testing the hypothesis that trust — conceived as a population-level propensity to cooperate — is especially consequential for the performance of large organizations. Their key theoretical argument is that cooperation in large organizations depends on trust more than cooperation in small, repeated-interaction settings (families, partnerships) does, because large organizations bring people together infrequently and offer too few interactions for reputation alone to sustain cooperation.

Using the World Values Survey measure of trust ("most people can be trusted") across 40 countries, they regress multiple outcomes — judicial efficiency, corruption, bureaucratic quality, tax compliance, civic participation, professional association membership, the sales share of the top 20 publicly-traded firms relative to GNP, infrastructure, infant mortality, educational attainment, inflation, and GDP growth — on trust and log per-capita GNP. A one-standard-deviation increase in trust (≈15 percentage points) corresponds to economically and statistically large improvements across nearly all outcomes. The large-firm result is particularly striking: higher trust increases the top-20 firm sales-to-GNP ratio by about 7 percentage points, consistent with Fukuyama's argument that trust substitutes for family ties in enabling large-scale corporate organization.

The paper also asks where trust comes from. Following Putnam, it tests the hypothesis that hierarchical religions discourage the horizontal networks that foster trust. The correlation between percent population belonging to a hierarchical religion (Catholic, Muslim, or Orthodox) and trust is −0.61, and religion predicts poor institutional outcomes even after controlling for income. Ethnolinguistic heterogeneity is not strongly correlated with trust (r = −0.12), suggesting that the religion channel is distinct from ethnic fractionalization.

## Key Extractions

1. "Trust is most needed to support cooperation in large organizations, where members interact with each other only infrequently because they are only rarely involved in joint production." (Section 1)
2. A one-standard-deviation increase in trust raises judicial efficiency by 0.7, anti-corruption score by 0.3, bureaucratic quality by 0.3, and tax compliance by 0.3 of a standard deviation, controlling for per-capita GNP.
3. Trust in people vs. trust in family: when both enter a regression predicting large-firm share of GNP, the coefficient on trust in people is +0.654 (t=4.1) and on trust in family is −0.563 (t=−3.1), confirming Fukuyama's hypothesis that family strength substitutes for impersonal trust but at the cost of firm scale.
4. Correlation between hierarchical religion and trust: −0.61 across 40 countries; driven primarily by the Catholic−trust correlation of −0.47.
5. "Theories of trust hold up remarkably well when tested on a cross-section of countries." (Conclusion)
6. The WVS trust correlation across the 1980s and 1990s waves is 0.91, suggesting high temporal stability of the measure.
