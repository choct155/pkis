## Summary

Malkiel (2003) surveys the major empirical challenges to the efficient markets hypothesis and defends a weakened but robust version of it. He defines efficiency pragmatically: markets do not allow investors to earn above-average returns without accepting above-average risks — a "no $100 bill on the ground" criterion. The paper reviews documented anomalies including short-term momentum, long-run mean reversion, seasonal effects (January effect, day-of-week), and valuation-ratio predictability (dividend yield, P/E ratios), evaluating each against the dual criteria of statistical robustness and economic exploitability.

Malkiel's primary conclusion is that most documented anomalies fail at least one of these criteria: either they are not stable across sub-periods (the January effect has largely disappeared), or they cannot survive transactions costs (momentum), or they reflect rational variation in risk premia rather than genuine inefficiency (value and size effects). The most direct evidence for efficiency, in his view, is the persistent failure of professional fund managers to beat passive index funds — three-quarters of actively managed funds underperform the S&P 500 index in any decade, and top-performing funds show no persistence across periods. The paper also addresses specific cases of apparent irrationality (1987 crash, Internet bubble, Palm/3Com spin-off) and argues that even these are consistent with rational responses to changed information or limit-to-arbitrage explanations that do not imply exploitable inefficiency.

## Key Knowledge Objects

- [[efficient-markets-hypothesis]] (concept, high) — the claim that markets do not allow risk-adjusted excess returns; reviewed and defended against behavioral critiques
- [[random-walk]] (concept, high) — the hypothesis that successive price changes are unpredictable; foundational to early EMH testing
- [[momentum-effect]] (result, moderate — could be concept) — short-horizon positive serial correlation in returns; inconsistent with strict random walk but insufficient for profitable trading after costs
- [[value-premium]] (result, high) — value stocks outperform growth stocks on average; surveyed and attributed to either risk or data mining
- [[size-effect]] (result, high) — small-cap stocks outperform large-cap stocks; questioned for post-1985 robustness
- [[survivorship-bias]] (concept, high) — upward bias in fund performance records from excluding failed funds; substantially inflates apparent active management returns
- [[limits-to-arbitrage]] (concept, moderate — could be framework) — constraints (short-sale limits, noise trader risk, horizon mismatch) that prevent rational arbitrageurs from eliminating mispricings
- [[behavioral-finance]] (framework, high) — research program attributing asset pricing anomalies to systematic investor psychological biases

## Key Extractions

1. Efficiency definition: "I will use as a definition of efficient financial markets that they do not allow investors to earn above-average returns without accepting above-average risks." Markets can be efficient even if they make valuation errors, if those errors are not exploitable.

2. On momentum: "Anyone who pays transactions costs is unlikely to fashion a trading strategy based on the kinds of momentum found in these studies that will beat a buy-and-hold strategy." (p. 7)

3. On the January effect: "Many predictable patterns seem to disappear after they are published in the finance literature... The so-called January effect, for example, seems to have disappeared soon after it was discovered." (p. 8–9)

4. On professional managers: "Throughout the past decade about three-quarters of actively managed funds have failed to beat the [S&P 500] index." (p. 32); "Those funds that produce excess returns in one period are not likely to do so in the next. There is no dependable persistence in performance." (p. 31)

5. On data mining: "Given enough time and massaging of data series, it is possible to tease almost any pattern out of most datasets... The published literature is likely to be biased in favor of reporting such results." (p. 22)

6. On limits to arbitrage during the Internet bubble: "Professional arbitrageurs will be loath to sell short a stock they believe is trading at two times its 'fundamental' value when it is always possible that some greater fools may be willing to pay three times the stock's value." (p. 28)
