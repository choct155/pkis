## Summary

Pagano's 2005 essay, written for a conference honoring Franco Modigliani, offers a clear and authoritative account of why the Modigliani-Miller theorems occupy a central place in financial economics — both for their substantive content and their methodological innovation.

On **substance**: MM theorems are "irrelevance propositions" — they identify a benchmark case where capital structure and dividend policy do not affect firm value. Their value lies not in proving these decisions are literally irrelevant, but in forcing analysts to identify precisely which real-world friction causes them to matter. The post-1958 development of corporate finance theory is essentially a systematic relaxation of MM's three key assumptions: (1) no taxes, (2) no bankruptcy costs, (3) perfect, symmetric capital markets. Relaxing the tax assumption (by MM themselves) implies a tax shield benefit to debt; relaxing bankruptcy costs creates a trade-off theory with an interior optimal leverage; relaxing informational symmetry (adverse selection, moral hazard) generates the vast modern literature on financial contracting, security design, and signaling. Pagano quotes Miller (1988): "showing what doesn't matter can also show, by implication, what does."

On **method**: The original MM proof used a clumsy "same risk class" comparison of two firms, but the core logic is arbitrage. The modern restatement is simple: (i) firm value = value of debt + value of equity; (ii) all cash flow goes either to debt or equity; (iii) the linearity of the no-arbitrage pricing rule means firm value equals the value of total cash flow regardless of how it is split. MM's use of arbitrage arguments — rather than equilibrium — was foundologically important. It became the standard method in derivatives pricing (Black-Scholes, put-call parity). Pagano notes that put-call parity is "really nothing more than MM Proposition I in only a mildly concealing disguise" (Miller, 1988).

The paper also notes MM's empirical humility and willingness to consider irrationality as an explanation for anomalies — anticipating behavioral finance before its time.

## Key Extractions

1. Irrelevance proposition as benchmark: "The virtue of this type of results does not lie in proving that the specified choice is truly irrelevant, but rather in forcing us to think hard about the assumptions that are necessary for it to be relevant."

2. Miller (1988) retrospective: "The view that capital structure is literally irrelevant or that 'nothing matters' in corporate finance … is far from what we ever actually said about the real world applications of our theoretical propositions … showing what doesn't matter can also show, by implication, what does."

3. Modern proof via linearity: "(i) the total value of a firm is the sum of the value of its debt and equity, (ii) the cash flow of the firm must go either to debt or equity, (iii) the linearity of the price rule implies that the sum of the values of debt and equity equals the value of the sum of its cash flow, irrespectively of how it is apportioned."

4. Post-MM program: "The entire development of corporate finance since 1958 … can be seen and described essentially as the sequential (or simultaneous) relaxation of the three assumptions" of no taxes, no bankruptcy costs, and perfect markets.

5. Arbitrage → Black-Scholes: "Black and Scholes (1973) relied on MM-type arbitrage arguments to derive their celebrated option pricing formula and … 'the familiar Put-Call Parity Theorem … is really nothing more than the MM Proposition I in only a mildly concealing disguise.'"

6. On behavioral finance: Pagano notes that Modigliani entertained seriously the possibility that manager irrationality explained deviations from MM-cum-taxes predictions — "he had 'become a bit disenchanted with the indiscriminate use of superrationality as the foundation for models of economic behavior.'"
