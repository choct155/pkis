## Summary

Merton Miller (1999) delivers a retrospective of modern finance through the lens of its Nobel Prize milestones, organized around a fundamental intellectual division he identifies in the field: the "business school" (micro normative) stream versus the "economics department" (macro normative) stream. The business school tradition trains decision-makers to optimize given prices; the economics tradition assumes a world of micro-optimizers and derives how prices evolve at the macro level. Modern finance emerged from the productive tension between these two streams.

Miller traces the field from Markowitz's 1952 portfolio selection (micro normative — a procedure for individual investors) through Sharpe's CAPM (macro normative transformation — deriving market equilibrium from Markowitz's assumptions) to the Modigliani-Miller propositions (macro normative — showing financing irrelevance in frictionless markets, with micro implications that were unwelcome to practitioners) and the EMH. He is characteristically blunt about the testability problems with M&M: confounding information effects make any capital structure event study uninterpretable. The Black-Scholes-Merton options pricing breakthrough is presented as the field's most transformative development because options prices are based on nearly observable quantities (variance is estimable from high-frequency data), potentially enabling a full reconstruction of finance on observable foundations. Miller's advice to young researchers in 1999: specialize in options research, leaving asset pricing and corporate finance to others.

## Key Extractions

1. "The characteristic business school approach tends to be what we would call in our jargon 'micro normative.' That is, a decision-maker... is seen as maximizing some objective function, be it utility, expected return, or shareholder value, taking the prices of securities in the market as given." (p. 95)

2. "What William Sharpe... [did] was transform the Markowitz business school model into an economics department model of enormous reach and power." Everyone holding the same Markowitz inputs implies everyone holds the market portfolio. (p. 97)

3. On M&M testability: "The firm, after all, never issues a press release saying 'we are just conducting a purely scientific investigation of the M&M propositions.' The market... has every reason to believe that the capital structure decisions are conveying management's views about changes in the firm's prospects for the future." (p. 99)

4. "The M&M propositions, in short, like the efficient markets hypothesis, are about equilibrium in the capital markets — what equilibrium looks like, and what forces are set in motion once it is disturbed." (p. 100)

5. On options' revolutionary nature: "For the first time in its close to fifty-year history, the field of finance can be built, or as I will argue be rebuilt, on the basis of 'observable' magnitudes." Variance is estimable from high-frequency data; means require decades of data. (p. 100)

6. "Every security can be thought of as a package of component Arrow-Debreu state-price contingent claims (options, for short), just as every physical object is a package of component atoms and molecules." (p. 101)
