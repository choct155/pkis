## Summary

Wray's 1998 Levy Institute working paper (later published in Smithin, ed., *What is Money?*, Routledge) argues that all modern economies operate with a "chartalist" or "state" money — a fiat currency whose value derives from the state's power to impose tax obligations payable in that currency, not from any commodity backing or barter origins. Drawing on Knapp, Keynes, Minsky, and Abba Lerner, Wray traces the intellectual lineage of the chartalist position from Adam Smith through the 20th century and links it to Lerner's "functional finance" framework.

The core analytical contribution is a sectoral accounting of how money enters circulation: government spending is the primary source of base money, and government deficits are the most important source of net money holdings in the non-government sector. This inverts conventional assumptions — fiscal policy, not monetary policy, drives the money supply. Monetary policy's actual function is to set the short-term interest rate by draining excess reserves created by deficit spending through bond sales (by Treasury and Central Bank combined). Bond sales are therefore not financing instruments; they are interest-rate management tools.

This reframing has significant policy implications: government cannot "run out of money" in its own currency, public debt is better understood as a record of private sector net financial assets, and bond sales do not crowd out private investment. The paper closes with policy proposals including employer-of-last-resort programs as a buffer-stock approach to full employment.

## Key Knowledge Objects

- [[chartalism]] (framework, high) — theory that money's value derives from state power to levy taxes, not from commodity backing or barter convention
- [[functional-finance]] (framework, high) — Lerner's doctrine that fiscal policy should be judged by its macroeconomic effects, not by analogy to household budgets
- [[state-theory-of-money]] (concept, high) — Knapp's theoretical claim that the state defines money by designating the medium in which taxes must be paid
- [[endogenous-money]] (concept, moderate — could be principle) — the proposition that money supply is determined by the economy's demand for credit/spending, not exogenously by the central bank
- [[employer-of-last-resort]] (concept, high) — government policy of guaranteeing employment at a fixed wage as a buffer-stock mechanism to achieve full employment and price stability

## Key Extractions

1. "The state 'defines' money when it chooses that in which taxes must be paid." (Wray paraphrasing Knapp/Keynes — the tax-driven theory of money acceptance.)

2. "Government spending is the most important determinant of the supply of base money, government deficits are the most important source of net money holdings." (Sectoral balance accounting identity.)

3. "Bond sales are not a part of fiscal policy, nor are they needed to 'finance' government deficits." (Bond sales are reserve-draining operations to hit the interest rate target, not financing operations.)

4. "Monetary policy consists of sales of government bonds (by the Treasury and Central Bank) to 'drain' excess reserves in order to hit the interest rate target established for monetary policy." (The operational mechanics of interest-rate targeting under a fiat system.)

5. "There is no evidence of barter-based markets (outside trivial prisoner-of-war cases), and all the evidence about the origins of money points to state involvement." (Empirical rejection of the Samuelsonian barter-origins narrative.)

6. Wray synthesizes the chartalist intellectual tradition: Knapp's *State Theory of Money* (1905), Keynes's endorsement in the *Treatise on Money* (1930), Minsky's financial instability framework, and Lerner's functional finance.

7. The employer-of-last-resort proposal uses the government as "employer of last resort" paying a fixed-wage buffer stock, absorbing labor during downturns and releasing it during booms — providing automatic stabilization without inflationary pressure.
