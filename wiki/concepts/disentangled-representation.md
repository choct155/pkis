## Definition
A learned representation $\mathbf{h}$ is disentangled if each coordinate (or direction) $h_i$ corresponds to a single, independently varying underlying causal factor of the data-generating process, so that changing $h_i$ alters only one aspect of the generated observation $\mathbf{x}$ while leaving all others intact.

Formally, if $\mathbf{x}$ is generated by $p(\mathbf{h},\mathbf{x}) = p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{h})\,p(\mathbf{h})$ with independent factors $h_i$, an ideal representation recovers this factorisation.

### Why it matters
Disentangled representations simplify downstream tasks: if $y$ is one of the causal factors $h_i$, predicting $y$ from $\mathbf{h}$ reduces to a nearly trivial linear problem. They also improve robustness to distribution shift because the causal mechanisms $p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{h})$ are invariant even when the marginal $p(\mathbf{h})$ changes across domains. Foundational motivation for VAEs, GANs, and a large body of representation-learning research.
