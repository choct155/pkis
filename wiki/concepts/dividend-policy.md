## Definition

Dividend policy is a firm's set of decisions about when and how much of its earnings to distribute to shareholders rather than reinvest. The key dimensions are: payout ratio (what fraction of earnings is distributed), form (cash dividends vs. share repurchases), and timing (regular quarterly dividends vs. special one-time distributions).

Under MM assumptions — perfect markets, no taxes, no asymmetric information — dividend policy is irrelevant. Investors can synthesize any desired income stream by selling shares ("homemade dividends"), so the split between distributions and retained earnings does not affect firm value. This dividend irrelevance theorem is the capital-distribution analog of MM Proposition I on capital structure.

## Post-MM Theory

The frictions that make dividend policy matter mirror those for capital structure:

- **Taxes**: differential taxation of dividends vs. capital gains creates clientele effects — investor groups sort into firms matching their tax situation, so each firm's policy implicitly targets a clientele.
- **Signaling**: managers with private information about future earnings use dividends as a costly signal. Unexpected dividend increases signal confidence; cuts signal distress. Empirically, unexpected changes are strongly associated with abnormal stock returns.
- **Agency costs**: Jensen (1986) argues that free cash flow — earnings in excess of positive-NPV investment opportunities — should be paid out to prevent managerial overinvestment. Dividends and debt serve as commitment devices that reduce the agency cost of free cash flow.

Jensen and Smith (1984) note that pre-1950 prescriptive finance had strong but theoretically unfounded views on optimal dividend policy. The post-MM research program converts these into positive questions about equilibrium policy under specific frictions.
