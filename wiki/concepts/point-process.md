## Definition
A point process is a model for a random distribution of points (atoms) in a state space E (typically a subset of [0,∞), ℝ, or ℝ^d). Given random points {X_n} in E, the associated random counting measure is N(·) = Σ_n ε_{X_n}(·), where ε_x is the Dirac (delta) measure putting mass 1 at x, so that N(A) = Σ_n ε_{X_n}(A) is the random number of points falling in a (Borel) set A. The defining technical (locally-finite / Radon) requirement is that every bounded set A contains finitely many points almost surely: P[N(A) < ∞] = 1. Multiplicities are handled by Σ_n ξ_n ε_{X_n}, where ξ_n ∈ {0,1,2,...} counts points stacked at site X_n. Rigorously, a point process is a measurable map N : (Ω, ℱ) → (M_p, 𝓜_p), where M_p is the space of locally-finite point measures m = Σ_i ε_{x_i} on E and 𝓜_p is the σ-field generated by the evaluation maps m ↦ m(I) for bounded rectangles I. The chief summary statistic is the mean measure (intensity) μ(A) = E N(A), the expected number of points in A. By Proposition 4.7.1, the finite-dimensional distributions p_{I_1,...,I_k}(n_1,...,n_k) = P[N(I_1)=n_1,...,N(I_k)=n_k] uniquely determine the law of N. Point processes underlie renewal processes, queueing arrival streams, spatial models (earthquakes, trees, galaxies), and extreme-value theory.
