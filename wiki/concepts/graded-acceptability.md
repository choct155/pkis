The empirically observed fact that linguistic acceptability is not binary (grammatical vs. ungrammatical) but exists on a gradient — some sentences are fully acceptable, some are partially acceptable with contextual repair, and some are wholly unacceptable; native speakers can make fine-grained distinctions across this spectrum. Murphy et al. (2025) test o3 on graded acceptability judgments and find that while it can identify egregiously unacceptable sentences, it struggles considerably with partially acceptable ones, classifying only 2 of 10 as partially acceptable. Classification note: assigned as concept but also_type principle because graded acceptability functions both as a defined linguistic phenomenon and as a guiding constraint on adequate theories of grammar.
