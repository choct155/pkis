## Definition
Sustenance is Pearl's aspect of causation that enriches dependence (necessity) with features of production (sufficiency) while remaining within a world u in which both cause x and effect y are true, thereby bridging Hall's "dependence" and "production" concepts without leaving the actual scenario. Definition 10.2.1: x causally sustains y in u relative to contingencies in a variable set W iff (i) X(u)=x, (ii) Y(u)=y, (iii) Y_{xw}(u)=y for all w (x alone suffices to maintain y even as W is set arbitrarily away from its actual value), and (iv) Y_{x'w'}(u)=y'!=y for some x'!=x and some w' (x bears responsibility: under some adverse setting of W, an alternative x' relinquishes y). Conditions (iii)-(iv) jointly imply there exists a setting in which x is both necessary and sufficient for y. Crucially, sustenance differs from ordinary dependence in the kind of contingencies x must protect y against: not circumstantial contingencies (other settings of U=u specific to the scenario) but structural contingencies arising from interventions (do-operator "miracles") that modify the model's mechanisms themselves. Sustenance includes the counterfactual necessity (but-for) test as the special case W=empty, and Pearl proposes it as the property that should replace the but-for test in multistage scenarios with multiple potential causes (preemption, overdetermination).
