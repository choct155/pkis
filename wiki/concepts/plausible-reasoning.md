## Definition
Plausible reasoning is the drawing of graded, uncertain conclusions from incomplete information, in contrast to deductive reasoning, which draws certain conclusions from complete premises. Jaynes frames it as the everyday process — concealed under the name *common sense* — by which we evaluate not merely the direction (more or less plausible) but the *degree* of plausibility of a proposition, drawing on both the specific new data and prior information.

A canonical motivating instance is the weak syllogism: $$\text{if } A \Rightarrow B,\ \text{and } B \text{ is observed true, then } A \text{ becomes more plausible.}$$ The conclusion is not logically forced, yet it carries real, sometimes near-certain, conviction. One-line intuition: it is logic stretched to operate on degrees of belief rather than on truth values alone.

### Why it matters
Plausible reasoning is the explanandum of the entire Jaynes program: the goal is to show that a unique quantitative theory (probability as extended logic) governs it, replacing conflicting intuitions and ad hoc procedures with definite theorems. It is the bridge between informal human inference and the Bayesian calculus.

### Relation to deductive logic
It is not a *weakened* logic but an *extension*: its rules contain deductive logic as a special case (the certain limits), while adding content — handling the cases where $A$ is false or $B$ is true — about which strong syllogisms say nothing.

Jaynes traces the lineage to Pólya, who catalogued the qualitative rules of plausible reasoning, and shows how those principles can be made quantitative.
