## Definition
An operational test for machine intelligence proposed by Alan Turing (1950) to sidestep the philosophically vague question "Can a machine think?". A computer **passes** if a human interrogator, after a written question-and-answer exchange, cannot reliably tell whether the responses came from a person or a machine.

The move is to replace an unanswerable definitional question with a behavioral, externally checkable criterion — intelligence judged by indistinguishable performance rather than by internal mechanism.

### Capabilities it implies
Programming a machine to pass requires natural language processing, knowledge representation, automated reasoning, and machine learning. The **total Turing test** adds physical interaction, requiring computer vision and robotics. These six disciplines compose most of AI.

### Why it matters
The test gave AI a concrete, falsifiable target and enumerated the field's core subdisciplines. It also crystallizes the "acting humanly" stance within the four approaches to AI.

### Why the field largely set it aside
Most researchers have devoted little effort to passing it, preferring to study the underlying principles of intelligence. Russell and Norvig give the analogy of artificial flight: aeronautical engineering succeeded by studying aerodynamics in wind tunnels, not by building machines that fly "so exactly like pigeons that they can fool even other pigeons." Imitating humans is neither necessary nor the most productive goal.
