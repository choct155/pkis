## Definition
A **$\sigma$-field** $\mathcal{F}$ on a sample space $\Omega$ is a collection of subsets closed under complement and countable unions/intersections, with $\emptyset, \Omega \in \mathcal{F}$. The **Borel $\sigma$-field** $\mathcal{B}$ on $\mathbb{R}$ is the smallest $\sigma$-field generated by all semi-closed intervals $(-\infty, b]$, and hence contains all open sets, closed sets, and countable intersections thereof:
$$\mathcal{B} = \sigma\!\left(\{(-\infty, b] : b \in \mathbb{R}\}\right).$$
It is the standard event space for continuous random variables on $\mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{R}^n$).

### Why it matters
Without a $\sigma$-field, probabilities cannot be consistently assigned to all "reasonable" subsets of a continuous space. The Borel $\sigma$-field is the canonical choice that makes the probability measure well-defined for all intervals, enabling integration-based probability calculations via the pdf.
