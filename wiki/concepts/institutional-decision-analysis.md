## Definition
## Definition
Institutional decision analysis is Gelman's term for the **objective** mode of using Bayesian inference in decisions: transparently specifying a probability model, a utility function, and an inferential framework that together yield cost estimates and decision recommendations open to scrutiny. It is set against the *subjective* personal view, in which posteriors encode an analyst's private beliefs fed through a personal utility into a decision tree. Gelman criticizes the subjective program as having an element of circular reasoning — by tuning subjective inputs one can reach almost any desired decision — and as often harder than simply choosing the appealing action directly.

### Why it matters
In businesses, governments, and research organizations, decisions must be *justified*, not merely made. The institutional framing makes the assumptions, data, and model fit explicit so a recommendation can be 'taken apart' to reveal its sensitivity to inputs. In the radon example, fixing a single cost-benefit assumption ($R_{action}=4$ pCi/L) yields concrete county-by-county recommendations that no one believes hold for every household, yet are useful as a transparent uniform policy. The value lies less in any single recommended action than in clarifying the relation between assumptions and consequences — and in identifying model expansions that let more information enter the inference.
