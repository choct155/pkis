The class of cognitive processes in which an agent constructs and internally "runs" a representation of a dynamic scenario — predicting outcomes by imagining how a scene unfolds over time. Mental simulation is a broader category than strict physics-engine simulation: it encompasses imagining trajectories, causal chains, and counterfactuals without necessarily requiring Newtonian mechanics. Davis and Marcus (2015) treat mental simulation as distinct from the "noisy Newton" variant (Battaglia et al.; Sanborn et al.) that requires full physics-engine mechanics. They argue that even if some mental simulation exists, it operates alongside and in dependence on non-simulative mechanisms (qualitative reasoning, rules, analogy), and that setting up and interpreting any simulation itself requires non-simulative cognition.
