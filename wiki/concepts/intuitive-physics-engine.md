The hypothesis that humans possess a cognitive mechanism for physical reasoning that functions analogously to computational physics engines, running approximate probabilistic simulations (Monte Carlo sampling over physical trajectories) to make predictions about physical events. Proposed by Battaglia, Hamrick, and Tenenbaum (2013) based on experiments with towers of blocks and bouncing balls. Davis and Marcus (2015) critique this hypothesis as both too weak (simulation fails for many physical tasks, especially those with incomplete information or non-prediction goals) and too strong (humans make systematic errors that a correct physics engine would not produce).
