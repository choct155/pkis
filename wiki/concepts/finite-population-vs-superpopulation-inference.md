## Definition
A distinction between two kinds of estimands in design-based Bayesian analysis. **Superpopulation estimands** are functions of the model parameters omega (e.g. E[y_i | omega]); they describe the infinite hypothetical population generated by p(y | omega, x) and retain uncertainty even under a census. **Finite-population estimands** are functions of the complete-data vector y itself (e.g. the population mean ybar = (n/N) ybar_obs + ((N-n)/N) ybar_mis, or the finite-population average causal effect ybar^A - ybar^B); they are unobserved but in principle *observable* ('predictable'), and would be known exactly if all units were observed. The standard Bayesian computation proceeds in two steps: superpopulation inference, p(omega, phi | x, y_obs, I), then finite-population inference via posterior simulation of y_mis from p(y_mis | x, y_obs, I, omega, phi) — draws of y_mis are *multiple imputations*. When a large fraction of potential observations is observed, finite-population inferences about y_mis are more robust to model assumptions (additivity, linearity) than superpopulation inferences about omega, because they depend only on the conditional distribution at the realized x-values. The two coincide in large samples: for simple random sampling, ybar | y_obs is approximately N(ybar_obs, (1/n - 1/N) s^2_obs), the Bayesian justification of normal-theory survey inference; for completely randomized experiments, ybar^A - ybar^B is approximately N(ybar^A_obs - ybar^B_obs, (2/n)(s^2A_obs + s^2B_obs)), matching randomization-based results. Finite-population causal effects depend on the unidentified correlation corr(y^A_i, y^B_i | omega), but this sensitivity vanishes as N/n grows when the estimand reduces to a comparison of marginal parameters omega^A, omega^B.
