## Definition
Jaynes's central foundational thesis: a probability is not a physical property of an object or experiment (like mass or moment of inertia), but a description of a *state of knowledge*. There is no measurable 'physical probability' of heads for a coin, of a face for a die, or of a bridge hand. The frequentist axiom (e.g. Cramer: 'any random variable has a unique [frequency] distribution') treats frequencies as physical constants of the device; but the laws of mechanics show this is false. The outcome frequency of a tossed coin depends on *how it is tossed* (the distribution over initial conditions in phase space), not on the coin alone: a skilled tosser, exploiting conservation of angular momentum, can produce any frequency of heads at will, even with an honest coin, and can reverse the direction of a bias's influence by choosing among tossing methods A/B/C. Formally, asserting a physical probability p_H is equivalent to asserting that the phase-volume ratio Gamma(H)/Gamma(R) is independent of the location of the initial region R in phase space — 'an utterly unprecedented new theorem of mechanics' that is simply false. The argument generalizes via an *infinite regress*: once the mechanism of any experiment E is understood, the question 'what are the probabilities of outcomes O_k?' reduces to 'what are the probabilities of the initial conditions I_k?', i.e. to an antecedent experiment E'; the notion of physical probability retreats from one level to the next as knowledge advances, like a child's spook that recedes from inspection. Demanding that anyone asserting a physical probability define the exact circumstances under which it could be measured is what distinguishes the claim as science rather than theology. Probabilities in classical statistical mechanics, and (Jaynes argues) in quantum theory, likewise express incompleteness of human knowledge rather than irreducible physical chance.
