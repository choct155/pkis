## Definition
De Finetti's (1937) program to ground probability theory itself on the notion of *coherence*: one should assign and manipulate degrees of belief so that one cannot be made a sure loser in a system of bets based on them (no Dutch book). From this single premise the standard rules of probability are derived: any incoherent assignment admits a set of bets guaranteeing the bettor a loss in every outcome, so coherence forces the sum and product rules.

Jaynes (Appendix A) acknowledges coherence as a near-relative of his own philosophy that would have shortened his derivations, but rejects it as a foundation on three grounds. (1) Aesthetic: it is inelegant to base the principles of logic on something as 'vulgar' as expectation of profit. (2) Strategic: defining probability via betting preferences focuses attention on eliciting the *personal* probabilities people happen to hold (a psychology problem), whereas extended logic concerns the probabilities one *ought to* hold given one's information, plus the principles (maximum entropy, transformation groups) for assigning priors from prior information. (3) Pragmatic and decisive: coherence is weaker than Cox-style *consistency*. Rules could be coherent yet inconsistent, giving no unique 'right answer' to a calculation; de Finetti never lists consistency as a desideratum. Jaynes shows that rules made to satisfy consistency automatically and trivially satisfy coherence, but not conversely. He also notes de Finetti's idiosyncratic treatment of additivity on uncountable sets leads to 'onion-layer' anomalies of differently-ordered zero probabilities summing to one, a source of the infinite-set paradoxes that motivate Jaynes's cautious finite-sets policy.
