## Definition
Jaynes' resolution of the old quarrel over 'final causes' (purpose) in nature: the error in nearly all prior discussion is the tacit assumption that a final cause must control *microscopic* detail — suspending physical law to steer individual molecules toward a desired end state. A **macroscopic** final cause does no such thing. The author writing a book, the chemist fixing volume and temperature, the bricklayer, the cook — each wields a genuine final cause that dictates a coarse-grained final condition while leaving the microscopic details utterly free. Had those fine details been different in any of billions of ways, the purpose would have been satisfied equally well.

## Statement
A macroscopic final cause therefore possesses an **entropy**: the (log) number of microscopic configurations consistent with its purpose. The larger that entropy, the greater the probability the purpose will be realized — so the **maximum entropy principle applies to teleology** exactly as it does to thermodynamic equilibrium and to probabilistic inference. The wielder of a final cause is not contravening physical law; he is merely *choosing the Hamiltonian* under which the system then evolves according to physical law.

## Why it matters
This dissolves a centuries-old confusion by separating two ideas that had been conflated: purpose at the macroscopic level (a familiar, real, daily phenomenon with no supernatural content) versus control of microscopic detail (the impossible kind that scientists rightly reject). It is a striking instance of the same maximum-entropy reasoning that underlies Jaynes' priors and his statistical mechanics, transplanted to the philosophy of purpose — and a concrete application of his recurring target, the mind-projection fallacy. The realized macrostate is the most probable one precisely because it is the one occupiable by the most microstates.
