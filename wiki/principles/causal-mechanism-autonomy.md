## Definition
The principle (which Pearl traces to early econometricians' notion of *autonomy*) that each structural equation in a causal model corresponds to an independent physical mechanism that can be changed without altering the others. This invariance is the source of a causal diagram's distinctive representational power: because the building blocks remain unaltered under intervention, a model assembled from autonomous equations can predict the consequences of interventions and abnormal manipulations that its designer never anticipated. Autonomy is precisely what is lost when structural equations are rewritten into algebraically equivalent (but mechanism-agnostic) form, and it is what licenses 'surgery' on a single equation while holding the rest fixed. Autonomy is the conceptual hinge that distinguishes a causal model from a merely equational or correlational one, and it underlies why attaining a causal model feels like 'deep understanding' and 'being in control.'
