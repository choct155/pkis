## Definition
Jaynes's distinctive thesis on the foundations of decision theory. Since the optimal decision depends only on the *product* $g(\alpha)L(\alpha,\beta)$ of prior and loss function, any apparent arbitrariness in the prior is matched by exactly equal arbitrariness in the loss function — so the sampling theorist's 'lopsided' worry about priors (while freely pulling loss functions out of thin air) is incoherent. Jaynes argues that, contrary to the 1950s Wald-era consensus, **priors are more firmly grounded than loss functions**: we have formal principles — maximum entropy, transformation groups, marginalization — that remove the arbitrariness of priors for large classes of problems, but *no* formal principles for assigning loss/utility functions (even the utility of money remains ill-defined). A further asymmetry concerns objectivity: it is a near-principle of morality that beliefs should not be swayed by desires (priors independent of wants), yet what we *want* legitimately depends on what we *know* — so a loss function is itself only a way of describing prior information about consequences. This motivates Jaynes's speculation about future 'multidimensional' (plausibility, value) representations of mental state that need not resemble present decision theory, and his preference for Cox's logical-consistency foundation over de Finetti's coherence/betting and Wald's expectation-primary approaches.
