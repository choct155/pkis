## Definition
The hypothesis of Newell and Simon (1976) that "a physical symbol system has the necessary and sufficient means for general intelligent action." The claim: any system — human or machine — that exhibits general intelligence must do so by manipulating data structures composed of symbols, and conversely, a system that manipulates symbols appropriately suffices for intelligence.

It elevates an empirical observation (programs like GPS and the Logic Theorist that solved problems by symbol manipulation also tracked human problem-solving) into a foundational principle about the nature of intelligence itself.

### Why it matters
The hypothesis is the philosophical charter of symbolic ("good old-fashioned") AI. It justifies the research program of building intelligence out of explicit representations and rule-governed manipulation, and it ties AI directly to the "thinking humanly" tradition of cognitive modeling.

### Status and challenges
Russell and Norvig note the hypothesis "has been challenged from many directions." Sub-symbolic and connectionist work — from perceptrons through modern deep learning — produces intelligent-seeming behavior without discrete symbol manipulation as the primitive, casting doubt on the *necessity* claim. The hypothesis remains a useful reference point for debates about symbolic vs. neural foundations of intelligence.
