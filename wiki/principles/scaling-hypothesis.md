The scaling hypothesis is the claim that scaling neural network models (larger architectures trained on more data with more compute) is sufficient to achieve general-purpose intelligence — the view that emergent reasoning, knowledge, and reliability will arise from scale alone; Belle and Marcus (2025) argue against this position, citing LLMs' brittle symbolic reasoning, planning failures, and confabulation as evidence that scale cannot substitute for explicit world models and symbolic reasoning mechanisms.
