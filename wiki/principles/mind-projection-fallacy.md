## Definition
The mind projection fallacy is the error of treating one's own state of knowledge — an epistemological quantity — as if it were a property existing externally in Nature (an ontological quantity). Jaynes names it from the observation that ordinary language disguises epistemological statements in grammatical forms that suggest ontological ones: 'The room is noisy' (a perception) reads like 'There is noise in the room' (an existence claim), a distinction some languages mark with different words.

One-line intuition: do not project the contents of your mind onto the world; *probability describes what you know, not what exists*.

### Why it matters
Jaynes identifies the fallacy as a major source of error in probability theory: speaking of 'the probability' as a real physical attribute of a system, rather than a description of incomplete information, leads to paradoxes. He argues that supposed difficulties motivating multiple-valued logics, fuzzy sets, and interpretations of quantum theory often reduce, on inspection, to instances of this fallacy — calling for revised concepts rather than a new logic.

### Scope
The fallacy is not confined to statistics; Jaynes contends much philosophical and physical discourse is 'reduced to nonsense' by repeatedly committing it. Recognizing the epistemological/ontological distinction is therefore a prerequisite for clear probabilistic thinking.
