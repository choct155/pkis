## Definition
Wald's fundamental theorem characterizing the class of admissible decision rules. For discrete states of nature (with no impossible $\theta_j$ included in the enumeration), the class of admissible strategies is exactly the class of **Bayes strategies** — those that minimize expected loss over a posterior pdf. When the $\theta_j$ form a continuum, the admissible rules are the *proper* Bayes rules, i.e. Bayes rules derived from proper (normalizable) priors. Jaynes calls this 'the conclusion that was shocking to sampling theorists — including Wald himself, who had been a proponent of von Mises' collective theory of probability — and it was psychologically the main spark that touched off the Bayesian revolution in statistics.' To his everlasting credit, Wald (1950) had the intellectual honesty to name the admissible rules 'Bayes strategies'. The term 'complete class' is defined awkwardly; what Wald wanted is the set of all admissible rules, Berger's 'minimal complete class'. Jaynes derives the same conclusion more directly (a 'simple substitute' for the complete class theorem) by minimizing a prior-weighted average risk, which forces Bayes' theorem to appear.
