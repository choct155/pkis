The fraction of consumption the representative household would surrender to eliminate all aggregate fluctuations and live in a certainty-equivalent path; Lucas (1987) famously showed this cost is tiny (~0.05% of consumption) under standard CRRA utility, but the result is radically larger under Epstein-Zin preferences with high risk aversion — where it is captured by the second-order perturbation term (1/2 · V_{33,ss} · σ²) and can reach 25% of consumption — suggesting that business cycle stabilization has nontrivial welfare relevance.

Classification note: assigned as result but may also be concept because the welfare cost of the business cycle is a well-defined theoretical object studied independently of any specific calibration or estimation.
