## Definition
The mechanism that lets off-policy RL estimate values for a target policy $\pi$ from returns generated by a behavior policy $b$. Returns under $b$ have the wrong expectation, $\mathbb{E}[G_t \mid S_t = s] = v_b(s)$, so each return is reweighted by the **importance-sampling ratio**, the relative probability of its trajectory under the two policies:
$$\rho_{t:T-1} \doteq \prod_{k=t}^{T-1} \frac{\pi(A_k\mid S_k)}{b(A_k\mid S_k)}.$$
The unknown environment transition probabilities appear identically in numerator and denominator and cancel, so $\rho$ depends only on the two policies and the action sequence, giving $\mathbb{E}[\rho_{t:T-1} G_t \mid S_t = s] = v_\pi(s)$. Two estimators average the scaled returns: **ordinary importance sampling** uses a simple average, $V(s) = \frac{\sum_t \rho_{t:T(t)-1} G_t}{|\mathcal{T}(s)|}$ — unbiased but with possibly *infinite* variance (a single large $\rho$ can blow up the estimate, and trajectory loops can make the variance unbounded). **Weighted importance sampling** uses a weighted average, $V(s) = \frac{\sum_t \rho_{t:T(t)-1} G_t}{\sum_t \rho_{t:T(t)-1}}$ — biased (bias $\to 0$ asymptotically) but with bounded, dramatically lower variance, and is strongly preferred in practice. An incremental update keeps a running cumulative weight $C$ and applies $V_{n+1} = V_n + \frac{W_n}{C_n}[G_n - V_n]$. Variance-reduction refinements (discounting-aware and per-decision importance sampling) exploit the return's structure as a sum of discounted rewards so that later, irrelevant ratio factors (expected value 1) do not inflate variance.

### Why it matters
Almost every off-policy method rests on importance sampling, so this is the workhorse that makes the powerful off-policy setting tractable. It also crystallizes a central practical tension in RL — the bias-variance tradeoff between ordinary (unbiased, high/infinite variance) and weighted (biased, low variance) estimators — and the loop/long-horizon variance pathologies it exposes directly motivate per-decision and discounting-aware corrections, and ultimately the move to temporal-difference methods in the following chapter.
