## Definition
The study of aggregating individual preferences into a collective decision, the domain underlying voting procedures. Voters hold qualitative preference orders over outcomes (ω ≻_i ω'); a *social welfare function* combines these into a collective ranking ≻*, while a *social choice function* outputs only a winner set. Condorcet's paradox shows aggregation can be intransitive (a majority cycle with no Condorcet winner). Desirable properties of an aggregation rule include the Pareto condition, the Condorcet-winner condition, independence of irrelevant alternatives (IIA), and no dictatorship. Concrete procedures include plurality, Borda count, approval voting, instant-runoff, and (true) majority rule. Two central impossibility results — Arrow's theorem and the Gibbard–Satterthwaite theorem — bound what any such rule can achieve.
