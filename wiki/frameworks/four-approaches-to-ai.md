## Definition
A 2x2 taxonomy that organizes historical conceptions of artificial intelligence along two orthogonal axes: the *standard of success* (fidelity to **human** behavior vs. an abstract, formal **rationality** — doing the "right thing") and the *object of study* (internal **thought processes** vs. external **behavior**). The four cells are: thinking humanly, thinking rationally, acting humanly, and acting rationally.

The schema names a fork in methodology: the human cells make AI an empirical science tied to psychology (observation, introspection, brain imaging), while the rational cells make it a formal discipline drawing on logic, mathematics, statistics, control theory, and economics.

### Why it matters
The taxonomy clarifies what otherwise looks like a single field pursuing one goal. Early AI conflated "is a good model of human performance" with "performs the task well"; separating the four claims let AI and cognitive science develop independently and faster.

### Structure
- **Thinking humanly** — cognitive modeling; programs whose reasoning steps match human protocols (e.g., GPS).
- **Acting humanly** — the Turing test; behavioral indistinguishability from a person.
- **Thinking rationally** — the "laws of thought"; logic and probability as correct inference.
- **Acting rationally** — the rational-agent paradigm: act to achieve the best (expected) outcome.

### Which cell wins
Russell and Norvig argue for **acting rationally**: it is more general (correct inference is just one route to good action) and more amenable to scientific development, since rationality is mathematically well defined. This is the organizing stance of the textbook and of much of the field.
